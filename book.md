# Intelligence?

*A Natural History of Mind Across the Animal Kingdom*

---

## One-sentence description

A natural history of mind that walks phylogenetically from bacteria through bilaterians, fish, insects, birds, mammals, and primates — examining each cognitive capacity (learning, memory, emotion, planning, self-awareness, language, creativity, theory of mind) by defining it rigorously and asking who passes the test under each definition, with artificial intelligence slotted into the same ladder as a data point in the penultimate chapter.

## Audience

Readers of *A Brief History of Intelligence* (Bennett), *Other Minds* (Godfrey-Smith), *Are We Smart Enough to Know How Smart Animals Are?* (de Waal), and *The Extended Mind* (Annie Murphy Paul). Scientists, educators, and thoughtful general readers who want a rigorous but readable account of what cognitive science and comparative biology have actually established — and what remains genuinely open. The book assumes no prior coursework. It does assume curiosity, patience for distinctions, and a willingness to revise intuitions about what humans share and do not share with worms, bees, dogs, octopuses, and machines.

## Scope

In: a survey of working definitions of intelligence; the minimum requirements for goal-directed behavior in agents without neurons; the phylogenetic emergence of learning, memory, emotion, pattern recognition, navigation, prediction, planning, social cognition, theory of mind, creativity, self-awareness, metacognition, language, and collective intelligence; a single chapter slotting AI into the same comparative framework; a closing argument about cognitive extension that runs quietly through every chapter and is named only in the final pages.

Out: metaphysics of consciousness as primary subject (touched only when an empirical question forces it); detailed neuroanatomy beyond what serves the comparative argument; IQ politics as a topic in itself; predictions about future AI capability beyond what current evidence supports; sentimental anthropomorphism, even where the organism is charismatic.

## Prerequisites

None. High-school biology, comfort reading the occasional equation, willingness to revise intuitions.

## Voice notes (book-specific)

- **Register:** Attenborough-inflected natural history with a philosophical spine. Precise, empirical, alive with the strangeness of organisms — and committed to naming when the evidence does not yet settle a question.
- **Comparable voices:** Godfrey-Smith on octopuses; de Waal on chimpanzees; David Attenborough on anything; Feynman where mechanism is being unpacked.
- **The question mark in the title is the thesis.** Every claim about what intelligence "is" is followed back to the operational test that would settle it. When the test is not yet possible, that fact is named.
- **Structural device — the Extension Note.** Each chapter introduces a cognitive capacity in some organism, and ends with a brief note on the human-built tools that extend that capacity beyond biological limits — the microscope extending perception, GPS extending navigation, the Roomba extending the worm's steering, written records extending memory. The leitmotif accumulates without commentary until Chapter 18 names it.
- **Each capacity is defined rigorously, often in multiple competing ways.** Under one definition of emotion, bees have it. Under another, they don't. Both answers are informative. The reader is shown the evidence and asked to decide.
- **First person used to label position-taking, not to perform humility.** "I conclude," "my reading is," "I do not yet understand."
- **No textbook scaffolding.** No Learning Objectives, no Prerequisites lists, no "Where this chapter fits." This is a trade book in the natural-history tradition. The chapters teach by example, not by curriculum.

## Chapter cadence

Three parts. Part One (Foundations, Ch. 1–2) sets the definitional problem and walks back to intelligence-without-neurons in bacteria, slime molds, and plants. Part Two (The Phylogenetic Ladder, Ch. 3–16) traces the emergence of each cognitive capacity in turn, organized by the earliest organisms in which the capacity is demonstrable. Part Three (The Argument, Ch. 17–18 + Epilogue) slots AI into the same framework and names the leitmotif that has been running through every Extension Note. A reader can read straight through, skip to a single capacity, or read the AI chapter alone — the comparative frame is set in Ch. 1.

## Running examples

- *Caenorhabditis elegans* and its 302-neuron nervous system — first complete connectome, the minimum case (introduced in Ch. 3, returned to in the Epilogue).
- The bumblebees of Lars Chittka's lab — for emotion, learning, and the limits of operational definitions.
- Chaser the border collie (Pilley & Reid 2011) — vertebrate vocabulary and category formation.
- New Caledonian crows — tool manufacture without prior demonstration.
- *Octopus vulgaris* and *Octopus cyanea* — distributed cognition and real-time camouflage as creativity.
- Kanzi the bonobo — the limits of ape language acquisition.
- Deep Blue (1997), AlphaGo (2016), and current LLM benchmarks — AI as data point in Ch. 17.

## Hero image direction

Natural-history register. Microscopy, behavioral footage stills, anatomical drawings in the lineage of Haeckel and Wilson. Avoid stylized "glowing brain" or "humanoid robot" visuals. Where AI is illustrated, prefer game positions, decision boundaries, or actual interface screenshots over abstract neural-net iconography.
