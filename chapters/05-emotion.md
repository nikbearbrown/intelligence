# Chapter 5 — Emotion
*Under one definition, bees have it; under another, they don't — and which definition you choose determines everything that follows.*

---

Somewhere in a laboratory in Newcastle, England, in 2011, there was a bee inside a plastic tube.

The tube was sealed. The air was fine. But the machine holding the tube was shaking it — hard, rhythmically, for sixty seconds. The shaking approximated what a bee experiences inside a heron's beak or a spider's grip: the world has gone wrong in a physically alarming way, the outcome is uncertain, and there is nothing to be done about it right now.

After sixty seconds, the tube stopped. The bee was released into an experimental arena. It had been trained, in the days before, to associate one odor with sugar water and a different odor with quinine — bitter, aversive, learned as bad. Now it was presented with a third odor, one that lay on the continuum between the two trained odors. An ambiguous smell. Could be good. Could be bad.

Bees from the unshaken control group responded in a sensible gradient: the closer the test odor was to the positive training odor, the more often they extended their proboscises to taste it. Bees from the shaken group were different. They were significantly less likely to extend toward the ambiguous odor, especially when it lay near the negative end of the continuum.

<!-- → [CHART: Proboscis extension rate (y-axis, 0–100%) vs. test odor position on the CS+/CS− continuum (x-axis, five positions) for shaken vs. unshaken bees — two lines showing the gradient response, with the shaken group depressed especially at the ambiguous midpoint and near-negative positions; reader should see the pessimistic shift, not just an overall suppression] -->

The shaking had not changed what the odors smelled like. It had not damaged their ability to taste, or their memory of which odors were safe. What had changed was the direction in which they resolved uncertainty.

In a human clinical context, this pattern — a bias toward negative interpretations of ambiguous information, persisting well after a stressful event — is called a symptom of anxiety. In a bee with a brain the size of a sesame seed, we have to decide what to call it. That decision is this chapter.

---

The word *emotion* is doing three different jobs at once in ordinary usage, and pulling them apart is the only way to do honest science here.

The first job: emotion as *feeling*. This is the one you have direct access to when you are frightened or delighted. It is the inner quality of the experience. It is also, notoriously, accessible only from the inside. The philosopher Thomas Nagel's question — *what is it like to be a bat?* — is not rhetorical. There is something it is like to be a bat *if* bats have subjective experience, and no amount of recording from bat neurons settles whether that condition is met. I will call this Definition Y.

The second job: emotion as *behavior* — the flinch, the freeze, the vocalizations, the facial configurations. Measurable. But a sign of an underlying state, not the state itself. A skilled actor displays grief without grieving.

The third job: emotion as *functional state* — a description of what a nervous system is doing that makes no claim about phenomenology. Defined by operational properties: how long it lasts, what it biases, what causes it, what stops it. This is the version I can actually investigate in a bee. Call it Definition X.

| Definition | What it describes | How it is measured | What it can and cannot settle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Definition Y — feeling / phenomenology | The felt, subjective experience of an emotion | Verbal report only (in humans); inaccessible elsewhere | Cannot be settled by any third-person measurement; not the working definition of this chapter |
| Emotion as behavior | A pattern of observable response (e.g. flight, freeze, approach) | Direct observation of behavior | Settles only what the organism *does*; cannot distinguish reflex from state |
| Definition X — functional state (Anderson–Adolphs) | An internal state with valence, persistence, scalability, generalization, and coordination | Cognitive-bias paradigm, persistence assays, dose–response, cross-modality probes | Settles whether the organism has the *functional architecture* of emotion; remains silent on phenomenology |

In 2014, David Anderson and Ralph Adolphs proposed five criteria for what a nervous-system state must satisfy before we are entitled to call it emotional in this functional sense. Each is independently testable.

*Persistence*: the state outlasts the stimulus that triggered it. A purely reflexive response ends when the stimulus ends. A functional emotional state continues.

*Scalability*: the intensity of the state scales with the intensity of the trigger. Small threat, mild state. Large threat, strong state.

*Valence*: the state is intrinsically positive or negative — something the system behaves as if it wants more of, or wants to escape. This is what makes a functional state *emotional* rather than merely arousal.

*Generalization*: the state biases responses across subsequent situations, including situations unrelated to the original trigger. Generalization is what makes an emotional state a *prior* on the world rather than a response to a specific stimulus.

*Coordination*: the state changes physiology, behavior, and cognition simultaneously, in a coherent direction.

Notice what this list does not include: consciousness, feeling, linguistic self-report. The Anderson-Adolphs criteria describe functional architecture, not phenomenology. They were designed so that neuroscientists can investigate the question empirically in nonhuman animals. And under this framework, the question is not whether bees have emotions in your sense — it is whether they have states that satisfy this description. Those are different questions, and they have different answers.

---

Now let me tell you why the behavioral test that applies these criteria took some years to arrive at, because the logic behind it is beautiful.

Researchers interested in animal affect had a traditional toolkit: measure physiological correlates like heart rate and cortisol, observe approach-avoidance behavior, record vocalizations. All of these carry information. All of them also carry ambiguity. A raised heart rate means something is happening; it does not cleanly distinguish a valenced emotional state from non-valenced arousal.

The breakthrough, formalized by Mike Mendl, Oliver Burman, and Elizabeth Paul around 2009, was this: a valenced affective state is, functionally, a *prior* on the world. An animal in a negative affective state is one whose expectations are shifted toward danger, loss, and aversive outcomes. An animal in a positive affective state expects reward, safety, appetitive outcomes. If this is true — and it follows directly from what a functional state *is* — then affective state should be detectable in how an animal interprets genuinely ambiguous information.

Present an animal with a stimulus that lies between a trained-positive and a trained-negative exemplar, one it has no prior experience with, and observe which way the interpretation tilts. A negatively-biased animal should interpret it as bad more often. A positively-biased animal should interpret it as good.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: The cognitive-bias paradigm — left panel shows training phase: CS+ stimulus paired with reward, CS− paired with aversive outcome; right panel shows test phase: ambiguous stimulus positioned on the CS+/CS− continuum, with two outcome arrows labeled "negatively biased animal" (interprets as CS−) and "positively biased animal" (interprets as CS+); reader should see that the paradigm requires a trained discrimination before the ambiguous test has any meaning] -->

This is what Melissa Bateson did to the shaken bees. The shaking was the manipulation. The ambiguous odor was the test. The direction of interpretation was the measurement. And it is what makes the result interpretable: the paradigm does not require the bee to tell you how it feels. It asks the bee a question — what is this? — and the answer reveals the prior.

---

The shaken-bee result established a pessimistic bias. The Perry, Baciadonna, and Chittka result in *Science* in 2016 established the opposite: a positive event induces an optimistic bias in bumblebees, and the mechanism involves dopamine. I want to walk through this experiment carefully, because it is the strongest mechanistic result in the chapter and it is worth understanding precisely.

Bumblebees were trained to associate one color of cylinder with a 40% sucrose solution and a different color with quinine. After training, bees reliably approached the rewarded color and avoided the aversive one. Standard differential conditioning.

Then, just before being released into the test arena, half the bees received an unexpected drop of 60% sucrose — a concentrated sweet surprise, above the training concentration. The other half received a water droplet as a control. The surprise was delivered in a staging area, not in the test arena, so it carried no information about what was coming.

The test arena contained cylinders of an intermediate color — one the bees had never encountered, carrying no learned association. Would bees that received the sucrose surprise approach it faster?

Yes. Bees that had received the sucrose reached the ambiguous cylinder significantly faster than controls. They also showed fewer hesitations en route. A pleasant surprise had biased their interpretation of an ambiguous stimulus toward positive.

Then Perry and colleagues added the critical experiment. A subset of bees received an injection of fluphenazine — a dopamine receptor antagonist — before the surprise sucrose. The fluphenazine did not prevent them from consuming the sucrose. It did not impair their locomotion. But it completely abolished the optimistic bias. Bees who received sucrose and fluphenazine behaved identically to the controls who received no sucrose at all.

<!-- → [CHART: Approach latency to the ambiguous cylinder (y-axis, seconds) for three groups — control (water + no drug), sucrose surprise (sucrose + no drug), sucrose + fluphenazine — bar chart showing sucrose group significantly faster than both other groups, which are statistically indistinguishable; reader should see the pharmacological dissociation: the drug restores baseline behavior despite the sucrose experience being intact] -->

This is what a mechanism looks like. Not just a behavioral correlation, but a pharmacological dissociation: block dopamine processing, and the affective shift that follows a pleasant surprise disappears, even though the sensory event itself was fully experienced. Apply the Anderson-Adolphs criteria: the state was persistent (expressed after the bee was transferred to a different arena), scalable (dose-dependent in subsequent work), valenced (positive, triggered by a pleasant event), generalized (biased responses to a stimulus completely unrelated to the original sucrose), and coordinated (affecting both decision speed and hesitation frequency simultaneously). All five. The mechanism involves dopamine in the insect brain — the same neuromodulator performing reward-signaling functions in *C. elegans*, in sea-slug learning, in vertebrate reward systems. The architecture is ancient. The function is conserved.

---

I want to be honest about where this result is clean and where it remains contested, because the disagreement matters.

For decades, the dominant view in insect neuroscience was that octopamine — structurally related to vertebrate norepinephrine — was the primary reward signal in insects. Dopamine in invertebrates was thought to be primarily associated with *aversive* signaling, not reward. The Perry result complicated this picture. If dopamine blockade eliminates an optimistic bias triggered by a pleasant surprise, then dopamine is doing something positive in the bee's affective economy.

Critics, including work from Martin Giurfa's lab, argued that what Perry observed might be better described as *appetitive motivation* rather than *positive affect*: the sucrose surprise increases the bee's overall tendency to approach potential food sources, and this general increase in foraging motivation — not a genuinely emotional state — shifts interpretation of the ambiguous cylinder.

The distinction is subtle but real. Consider the difference between being in a good mood and being hungry. Both increase your tendency to approach food-associated stimuli. But mood affects how you interpret *non-food* stimuli too — an unrelated piece of music sounds better when you are happy. Hunger does not do that. If what the bee has is mood, the bias should generalize to non-food ambiguous stimuli. If what the bee has is appetitive motivation, the bias should be specific to food.

This specific test — presenting bees with an ambiguous stimulus that has nothing to do with food — has not, to my reading, been definitively done. The disagreement turns on exactly this prediction. Chittka's interpretation is that the fluphenazine dissociation already argues against a pure motivation account: blocking dopamine before the sucrose should reduce approach to *all* stimuli if motivation is the explanation, and that is not what was observed. Giurfa's reading is that the dissociation is less clean than Chittka claims.

| Test | Mood interpretation predicts | Appetitive-motivation interpretation predicts |
|---|---|---|
| Does the bias generalize to non-food ambiguous stimuli? | Yes — mood is a global state | No — appetitive motivation is reward-specific |
| Does dopamine antagonist affect all approach-related behaviors? | Yes — across-the-board attenuation | Only food-related approach is attenuated |
| Does the bias persist after the surprise reward is consumed? | Yes — mood persistence | No — return to baseline as motivation discharges |
| Open empirical question | Run a non-food generalization probe (e.g. shelter approach) under the same dopamine manipulation | The same experiment distinguishes the accounts |

I find the Chittka interpretation more parsimonious given the full body of evidence. But I want you to know this is a live debate, not a settled question. The architecture I am calling emotion in bees is a specific empirical position with specific critics and specific unresolved predictions.

---

Two other cases push the question in different directions and reveal what happens when the architecture and the phenomenology come apart.

Robert Elwood, at Queen's University Belfast, has published two decades of work on whether crustaceans experience pain in a functional sense. The most cited result: hermit crabs living in high-quality shells tolerate more electric shock before abandoning the shell than crabs living in poor shells. The crabs are trading off the badness of the shock against the goodness of what they stand to lose — using some common currency of value that a pure nociception account, predicting a fixed withdrawal threshold, cannot explain. All five Anderson-Adolphs criteria are met. In 2022, the United Kingdom formally recognized decapod crustaceans as sentient in the Animal Welfare (Sentience) Act, the first time invertebrates have received formal sentience recognition in UK law.

The second case is stranger. George Adrian Horridge demonstrated in 1962 that a *decapitated* cockroach — thoracic ganglion intact, brain removed — can learn to keep its leg raised to avoid a shock. Follow-up work confirmed that the same preparation, exposed to inescapable shocks, subsequently shows reduced escape attempts when escape becomes available: the learned-helpless signature, a well-established functional model for depression in mammals, appearing in tissue that contains no central brain.

Under Definition X, the headless cockroach has a functional state that meets the criteria for helplessness. Under Definition Y, the headless cockroach almost certainly does not have anything we could call *despair*, because there is no central integration that could be the what-it-is-like of the state. The two definitions diverge as sharply here as they can.

What I find most striking is what the cockroach implies about where functional emotion lives. If the learned-helpless state runs in a thoracic ganglion with no brain, then either the Anderson-Adolphs criteria are including something they should not, or functional emotional states are genuinely more distributed than we assume. The result does not mean the thoracic ganglion is conscious. It may mean we have been too quick to assume that emotion-as-functional-state requires the kind of centralized integration we associate with vertebrate brains. The bee's mushroom bodies — the insect equivalent of a higher integration center — appear to be where affective signals bind to sensory representations. Whether the bee's state is *richer* because it runs through that center, or merely differently expressed, is not settled.

| Criterion | Shaken honeybee (Bateson 2011) | Surprised bumblebee (Perry 2016) | Hermit crab (Elwood) | Headless cockroach (Horridge) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Persistence | ✓ — pessimistic bias outlasts the shaking | ✓ — optimism outlasts sucrose surprise | ✓ — withdrawal preference persists after shock | ? — duration not characterized |
| Scalability | ✓ — graded by shake intensity | ✓ — graded by sucrose magnitude | ✓ — graded by shock intensity | — |
| Valence | ✓ — negative | ✓ — positive | ✓ — negative | ? |
| Generalization | ? — limited cross-modal testing | ? — limited cross-modal testing | ✓ — preference shifts across new shells | — |
| Coordination | ✓ — physiological + behavioral | ✓ — dopamine-mediated multi-system | ✓ — multi-system | — |

---

The two large formal declarations of recent years are worth knowing, both for what they claim and for the care with which they phrase those claims.

The *Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness* in 2012 stated that nonhuman animals "possess the neurological substrates that generate consciousness" — widely reported as declaring that animals are conscious, but actually claiming only that animals have the *structural prerequisites*, not that consciousness itself has been demonstrated.

The *New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness* in 2024 was more careful. It affirmed strong evidence for conscious experience in mammals and birds, then added, for invertebrates: "we believe that, for some invertebrates, such as insects, decapod crustaceans, and cephalopod molluscs, there is at least a realistic possibility of conscious experience."

*Realistic possibility* is doing real work in that sentence. It is not claiming that bees feel pessimism the way you feel anxious. It is claiming that the probability is high enough that ignoring it would be a moral error.

I think this is the right standard. The gap between Definition X and Definition Y may never close — not because science will fail to improve, but because Definition Y is asking a question that third-person methods may be structurally unable to answer. There may be no observation from outside a nervous system that tells you whether there is something it is like to be inside it. This is the hard problem of consciousness, and it is hard in the specific sense that no amount of functional evidence resolves it.

What the evidence *can* do is make Definition Y progressively less plausible to deny. A single neuromodulatory architecture conserved from worms to vertebrates; functional states meeting all five criteria; cognitive-bias results replicating across phylogenetically distant animals; integration centers binding sensory and affective information in insects as in mammals — this pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that something runs through the whole nervous system continuum. The hypothesis that there is a hard cutoff, above which there is experience and below which there is nothing, has no mechanism. It is a stipulation, not a theory.

I do not know whether the shaken bee felt anxious. I know that it had a state that did to its cognition what anxiety does to ours. That may not be the same thing. But it is not nothing.

---

Let me close by defending why the bee is the right case to have built this argument around.

The bee is small. The behavior is not breathtaking. A biased interpretation of an ambiguous odor does not have the drama of an elephant grieving or a chimpanzee using a tool. Why make so much of it?

Because the bee is precisely the case where the two definitions come apart most cleanly. If the subject were human anger, both definitions would apply and the interesting questions would be about degree and texture. If the subject were a robot displaying angry facial expressions, neither definition would apply and the question would be trivially settled.

The bee sits in the gap. The functional evidence is solid enough that dismissing it requires you to explicitly reject the Anderson-Adolphs criteria, or to argue that the criteria do not generalize to insects, and to give a principled reason why. The phenomenological evidence is weak enough that asserting subjective experience requires committing to a position you cannot test. The bee forces the choice between definitions into the open, where it can be examined rather than assumed.

As we move up the phylogeny — through fish, through birds, through dogs and primates — the phenomenological gap that looks so wide at the bee starts to narrow in ways that become harder to dismiss. But the argument's structure is the same at every level. What changes is the weight of the evidence.

The reader who wants to claim that only humans have emotions in the full Definition Y sense is entitled to that position. But they have to say exactly where on the phylogenetic ladder the capacity appears, and what mechanism produces it at that point and not before. The ladder has no rungs that are not connected to the rungs below them. The mechanism is conserved. The function is conserved. The claim that phenomenology appears suddenly, at some arbitrary point, without a conserved mechanism, is the claim that needs defending.

The shaken bee did not choose her pessimism. She did not deliberate. She did not decide that, given recent events, the world looked more dangerous than it had. The state arose from chemistry, and the chemistry changed what everything else meant. This is true of her. It is also true of us. The question is not whether that makes her less. It is whether it makes us different in kind, or only in degree.
