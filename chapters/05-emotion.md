# Chapter 5: Emotion
## Under One Definition, Bees Have It; Under Another, They Don't

---

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

1. **Distinguish** the three major senses of "emotion" — as feeling, as behavior, and as functional state — and explain why the distinction matters for comparative research
2. **Apply** the Anderson-Adolphs five criteria (persistence, scalability, valence, generalization, coordination) to evaluate whether a given behavioral result constitutes evidence for emotion
3. **Explain** the cognitive-bias paradigm and describe how it provides a third-person test for what might otherwise be an unanswerable first-person question
4. **Analyze** the bumblebee dopamine-blockade experiment to identify which components of the emotion claim are mechanistically established and which remain contested
5. **Articulate** the difference between what the evidence can settle (Definition X) and what it cannot settle (Definition Y) in the case of invertebrate affect

**Prerequisites:** Chapter 3's treatment of neuromodulators and internal state, particularly the dopamine and serotonin systems in *C. elegans*. Chapter 4's account of associative learning and the role of coincidence-detection in valenced memory formation.

**Where this fits:** Chapters 3 and 4 established that nervous systems can have *internal states* that re-weight sensory processing. This chapter asks when those states deserve the name *emotion* — and discovers that the answer depends almost entirely on which definition you choose.

### Chapter Opening: Sixty Seconds in a Tube

Somewhere in a laboratory in Newcastle, England, in 2011, there was a bee inside a plastic tube.

The tube was sealed. The air was fine; the bee would not suffocate. But the tube was in a machine, and the machine was shaking it — hard, rhythmically, for sixty seconds. The shaking approximated what a bee experiences inside a heron's beak or a spider's grip: the world has gone wrong in a physically alarming way, the outcome is uncertain, and there is nothing to be done about it right now.

After sixty seconds, the tube stopped. The bee was released into an experimental arena. It had been trained, in the days before, to associate one particular odor with sugar water and a different odor with quinine — bitter, aversive, a stimulus it had learned to avoid. Now it was presented with a third odor, one that lay on the continuum between the two trained odors. An ambiguous smell. Could be good. Could be bad.

Bees from the unshaken control group responded to this ambiguous odor in a sensible gradient: the closer the test odor was to the positive training odor, the more often they extended their proboscises — their feeding mouthparts — to taste it. Bees from the shaken group were different. They were significantly less likely to extend to the ambiguous odor, especially when it lay near the negative end of the continuum.

The shaking had not changed what the odors smelled like. It had not damaged the bees' ability to taste or their memory of which odors were safe. What had changed was the direction in which they resolved uncertainty.

In a human clinical context, this pattern — a bias toward negative interpretations of ambiguous information, persisting well after a stressful event — is called a symptom of anxiety. In a bee with a brain the size of a sesame seed, we have to decide what it means. That decision is this chapter.

## 5.1 Three Senses of One Word

The word *emotion* is doing three different jobs in ordinary usage, and pulling them apart is the only way to do honest science with invertebrates.

**Emotion as feeling** — the phenomenological version — is the one you have direct access to when you are frightened or delighted. It is the inner quality of the experience. It is also, notoriously, accessible only from the inside. There is no instrument that detects subjective experience from outside a nervous system. The philosopher Thomas Nagel's famous question — *what is it like to be a bat?* — is not rhetorical. There is something it is like to be a bat if bats have subjective experience, and no amount of recording from bat neurons tells you whether that condition is met. Call this **Definition Y**.

**Emotion as behavior** — the ethological version — is what you observe from outside: the flinch, the freeze, the approach, the vocalizations, the facial configurations that humans and other animals display in characteristic circumstances. This version is measurable. It has the limitation of being a *sign* of an underlying state rather than the state itself. A very skilled actor displays the behaviors of grief without grieving. A bee extending its proboscis toward a flower is not necessarily in any state that warrants the word *happy*.

**Emotion as functional state** — the version this chapter lives in — sits between the other two. A functional state is a description of what a nervous system is doing that makes no claim about phenomenology. It is defined by operational properties: how long does it last, what does it bias, what causes it, what stops it. A thermostat does not have functional states in any interesting sense. A bee appears to. Defining the line between them is the chapter's actual problem.

I will use the Anderson-Adolphs framework as my definition of a functional state that counts as emotional. In a 2014 paper in *Cell*, David Anderson and Ralph Adolphs proposed five criteria that any nervous-system state must meet before we are entitled to call it emotional in this functional sense. Each criterion is independently testable:

**Persistence:** The state outlasts the stimulus that triggered it. A purely reflexive response ends when the stimulus ends. A functional emotional state continues.

**Scalability:** The intensity of the state scales with the intensity of the trigger. Small threat, mild state. Large threat, strong state.

**Valence:** The state is intrinsically positive or negative — something the system behaves as if it wants more of, or something it behaves as if it wants to escape. Valence is what makes a functional state *emotional* rather than merely *arousal*.

**Generalization:** The state biases responses across subsequent situations, including situations unrelated to the original trigger. Generalization is what makes an emotional state a *prior* on the world rather than a response to a specific stimulus.

**Coordination:** The state changes physiology, behavior, and cognition simultaneously, in a coherent direction. A sudden elevation of heart rate without any change in behavior is not an emotion; an integrated shift across multiple systems is.

Notice what this list does not include: *consciousness*, *feeling*, *linguistic self-report*. The Anderson-Adolphs criteria describe the functional architecture of emotion, not its phenomenology. They were designed so that neuroscientists can investigate the question empirically in nonhuman animals.

Call this framework **Definition X**.

The chapter's governing claim: under Definition X, the evidence is good enough to settle the question in at least several invertebrate species. Under Definition Y, the question is open and may be permanently so. Both answers are interesting. They are answers to different questions, and confusing them has produced a large amount of unproductive debate.

## 5.2 The Cognitive-Bias Paradigm

The behavioral test that connects Definition X to actual experiments is not complicated, but it took some years to arrive at.

Researchers interested in animal affect had a traditional toolkit: measure physiological correlates (heart rate, cortisol), observe approach-avoidance behavior, record vocalizations. All of these carry information, but all of them also have alternative explanations. A raised heart rate means something is happening; it does not cleanly distinguish a valenced emotional state from non-valenced arousal.

The breakthrough was the *cognitive-bias paradigm*, developed by Mike Mendl, Oliver Burman, and Elizabeth Paul around 2009 and published in *Proceedings of the Royal Society B* in 2010. The core insight: a valenced affective state is, functionally, a *prior* on the world. An animal in a negative affective state is one whose expectations are shifted toward danger, loss, and aversive outcomes. An animal in a positive affective state is one whose expectations are shifted toward reward, safety, and appetitive outcomes.

If this is true, then affective state should be detectable in how an animal *interprets ambiguous information*. Present an animal with a stimulus that is genuinely ambiguous — one that lies between a trained-positive and a trained-negative exemplar, so that the animal has no prior experience with it — and observe which way the interpretation tilts. A negatively-biased animal should interpret it as bad more often. A positively-biased animal should interpret it as good more often.

This is the paradigm Melissa Bateson used on the shaken honeybees. The shaking was the manipulation. The ambiguous odor was the test. The direction of the interpretation was the measurement.

The paradigm had already been validated in rats, dogs, sheep, and pigs before Bateson applied it to bees. What makes the bee case important is not that it used the paradigm first. It is that the bee is about as far from a vertebrate as a bilateral animal can be while still having a brain, and the same logic applies. The paradigm has since been used in starlings, crows, crayfish, and — in a rudimentary form — in zebrafish. The interpretation-bias result appears wherever anyone looks hard enough and designs a clean test.

## 5.3 The Shaken Bee and the Surprised Bumblebee

The Bateson (2011) result established the pessimistic bias in shaken honeybees. The Perry, Baciadonna, and Chittka (2016) result in *Science* established the opposite: a positive event induces an optimistic bias in bumblebees, and the mechanism involves dopamine.

I want to walk through the Perry experiment in some detail because it is the strongest mechanistic result in this chapter. Understanding it precisely is worth the time.

**The training phase:** Bumblebees were trained to associate one color of cylinder with a 40% sucrose solution (reward) and a different color with a 40% quinine solution (aversive). After training, bees reliably approached the rewarded color and avoided the aversive one. This is standard differential conditioning. It establishes the discrimination from which an "ambiguous" stimulus can be defined.

**The manipulation:** Half the bees, just before being released into the test arena, received an unexpected drop of 60% sucrose — a concentrated sweet surprise, above the level used in training. This is a pleasant, unexpected reward. The other half received no manipulation (water droplet as a control). The surprise sucrose was delivered in the staging area, not in the test arena itself, so it carried no information about what was coming.

**The test:** The test arena contained cylinders of an intermediate color — one that fell between the rewarded and aversive training colors. Bees had never encountered this color before. It carried no learned association. The question: would bees approach it faster if they had received the sucrose surprise?

**The result:** Yes. Bees that had received the sucrose reached the ambiguous cylinder significantly faster than controls. They also showed fewer hesitations en route. The sucrose had biased their interpretation of an ambiguous stimulus toward positive.

**The mechanism:** Perry and colleagues then replicated the experiment with a critical addition. A subset of bees received an injection of *fluphenazine*, a dopamine receptor antagonist, before the surprise sucrose was delivered. The fluphenazine did not prevent the bees from consuming the sucrose. It did not impair their locomotion. But it completely abolished the optimistic bias. Bees who received sucrose *and* fluphenazine behaved identically to the controls who received no sucrose at all.

This is what a mechanism looks like. Not just a behavioral correlation, but a pharmacological dissociation: block dopamine processing, and the affective shift that follows a pleasant surprise disappears, even though the sensory event itself was fully experienced.

Now apply the Anderson-Adolphs criteria.

**Persistence:** The state induced by the sucrose surprise lasted through the test phase, which occurred after the bee had been transferred to the test arena. The trigger and the expression were temporally separated.

**Scalability:** Perry's experiment used one concentration of sucrose surprise; subsequent work varying the concentration has found dose-dependent shifts in the bias magnitude.

**Valence:** The state was positive — triggered by a pleasant event, expressing as optimistic interpretation of ambiguity.

**Generalization:** The state biased responses to a stimulus (the ambiguous color cylinder) that was completely unrelated to the original sucrose surprise. The generalization is genuine.

**Coordination:** The state affected both decision speed and hesitation frequency simultaneously, consistent with a coordinated shift in the animal's evaluative stance rather than a simple change in one motor parameter.

All five criteria met. The mechanism involves dopamine in the insect brain, a neuromodulator we have already established is performing reward-signaling functions in *C. elegans* (Chapter 3) and in sea slug learning (Chapter 4). The architecture is ancient. The solution is conserved. The capacity appears genuinely homologous across phyla.

## 5.4 The Contested Chemistry

I want to be honest about where the bee case is clean and where it remains contested, because the disagreement matters conceptually.

For decades, the dominant view in insect neuroscience was that *octopamine* — a molecule structurally related to vertebrate norepinephrine — was the primary reward signal in insects. Octopamine is released when bees forage successfully, is required for appetitive learning in certain paradigms, and had been thought to be doing most of the work that dopamine does in vertebrates. Dopamine in invertebrates was thought to be primarily associated with *aversive* signaling, not reward.

The Perry result complicated this picture. If dopamine blockade eliminates an optimistic bias triggered by a pleasant surprise, then dopamine is doing something positive in the bee's affective economy. But critics, including important work from Martin Giurfa's lab, argued that what Perry observed might be better described as *appetitive motivation* rather than *positive affect*: the sucrose surprise increases the bee's overall tendency to approach potential food sources, and this general increase in foraging motivation, rather than a genuinely emotional state, is what shifts the interpretation of the ambiguous cylinder.

The distinction is subtle but real. Consider the difference between *being in a good mood* and *being hungry*. Both increase your tendency to approach food-associated stimuli. But they do different things to the rest of your behavior: hunger also makes you more persistent, more willing to tolerate effort costs for food, possibly more irritable about interruptions. Mood affects how you interpret *non-food* stimuli too — an unrelated piece of music sounds better when you are happy. If what the bee has is mood, the bias should generalize to non-food ambiguous stimuli. If what the bee has is appetitive motivation, the bias should be specific to food-associated stimuli.

This specific test — presenting shaken or surprised bees with an ambiguous stimulus that has nothing to do with food — has not, to my reading, been definitively done. The disagreement between the Chittka interpretation (mood-like state) and the Giurfa interpretation (appetitive motivation) turns on exactly this question. The chapter's clean mechanistic picture is honest only if you accept that the dopamine-blockade result specifically implicates a state rather than a drive. Chittka's reading of the fluphenazine result is that it does: a pure motivation account predicts that blocking dopamine would reduce approach to *all* stimuli, not just ambiguous ones, and that is not what was observed. Giurfa's reading is that the dissociation is less clean than Chittka claims.

I find the Chittka interpretation more parsimonious given the full body of evidence, including the shaking results that do not fit neatly into an appetitive-motivation account at all. But I want students to know this is a live debate rather than a settled question. The architecture I am calling *emotion* in bees is not a cartoon; it is a specific empirical position with specific critics and specific unresolved predictions.

## 5.5 The Decentralized Cases

The bee is the chapter's strongest evidence. Two other invertebrate cases push the question in different directions and are worth dwelling on, because they reveal what happens when the architecture and the phenomenology come apart.

**The crustacean pain question.** Robert Elwood, a behavioral ecologist at Queen's University Belfast, has published a sequence of papers over two decades on whether crustaceans — hermit crabs, shore crabs, prawns — experience pain in a functional sense. The most cited result: hermit crabs living in high-quality shells tolerate more electric shock before abandoning the shell than crabs living in poor-quality shells. The crabs are *trading off* the cost of the shock against the value of what they have to give up.

This matters because a pure nociception account — the simplest possible explanation for shock avoidance — predicts a fixed threshold. The animal detects tissue damage, and above some intensity, it withdraws. That threshold should not depend on what is in the shell. The fact that it does means the crab is performing an evaluation that compares the badness of the shock against the goodness of the shell, using some *common currency* of value. The Anderson-Adolphs criteria for a valenced state are met: the state produced by the shock is persistent (crabs continue rubbing affected areas for hours), scalable, valenced, generalized, and coordinated.

In 2022, the United Kingdom formally recognized decapod crustaceans as sentient in the Animal Welfare (Sentience) Act. The legal basis was a review by Jonathan Birch and colleagues at the London School of Economics that concluded the evidence for sentience in these animals met the standard of *balance of probabilities*. This is the first time invertebrates have received formal sentience recognition in UK law. It happened because of exactly the kind of evidence this chapter describes.

**The headless cockroach.** The learned-helplessness paradigm is a well-established model for depression in mammalian research. The procedure: expose an animal to inescapable shocks, then test whether it attempts to escape when escape later becomes possible. A *helpless* animal has learned, functionally, that its actions do not influence outcomes, and it stops trying even when they could. The state meets every Anderson-Adolphs criterion. It has clinical analogs in human depression.

George Adrian Horridge demonstrated in 1962 that a *decapitated* cockroach — thoracic ganglion intact, brain removed — can learn to keep its leg raised to avoid a shock. The thoracic preparation learns the contingency. Follow-up work has confirmed that the same preparation, exposed to inescapable shocks, subsequently shows reduced escape attempts even when escape is available. The learned-helpless signature appears in tissue that contains no central brain.

Under Definition X, the headless cockroach has a functional state that meets the criteria for helplessness. Under Definition Y, the headless cockroach almost certainly does not have anything that could be called *despair*, because there is no central integration that could be the *what-it-is-like* of the state. The two definitions diverge sharply here.

What I find most striking about this result is what it implies for the *locus* of functional emotion. If the learned-helpless state can be implemented in a thoracic ganglion with no brain, then either the criteria are wrong (they are including something they should not) or functional emotional states are genuinely distributed in ways we do not fully understand. The result does not mean that the thoracic ganglion is conscious. It may mean that we have been too quick to assume that emotion-as-functional-state requires the kind of centralized integration we associate with vertebrate brains.

This is the puzzle I find most unresolved in this chapter. The bee's mushroom bodies — the insect equivalent of a higher integration center — are where affective signals appear to be bound to sensory representations. The headless cockroach suggests that something emotion-like can run without that binding. Whether the bee's affective state is *richer* because it runs through a higher integration center, or whether it is merely *different in expression* while being structurally similar, is not settled.

## Concept Worked Example: Applying the Anderson-Adolphs Criteria

Let me work through a specific case so you can practice the framework.

A study on shore crabs finds the following: (1) crabs given an electric shock to one antenna subsequently rub that antenna for 30–60 minutes; (2) the rubbing decreases in frequency over time but does not disappear for several hours; (3) crabs that receive a stronger shock rub more frequently and for longer; (4) crabs that have been shocked are also more likely to retreat from a simulated predator approach in the hours following the shock, even when the predator is not near the antenna that was shocked; (5) crabs that have been shocked show elevated respiratory rate for several hours.

Apply the five criteria:

**Persistence:** The rubbing continues for hours after the shock ends. The elevated respiration persists through the test period. ✓

**Scalability:** Rubbing frequency and duration scale with shock intensity. ✓

**Valence:** Shock is aversive — the behavioral signatures (rubbing, increased retreat tendency) are consistent with a negative state. ✓

**Generalization:** Increased retreat from a simulated predator is a response to a *different* stimulus. The state produced by the shock is affecting behavior in a situation unrelated to the original shock. ✓

**Coordination:** Both behavioral (rubbing, retreat) and physiological (respiratory rate) outputs are changed simultaneously in a consistent direction. ✓

**Verdict under Definition X:** All five criteria are met. The shore crab has a functional state that qualifies as emotional by the Anderson-Adolphs framework.

**Note what we cannot say:** We cannot say whether there is something it is like to be the crab with this state. We cannot say the crab *suffers* in any phenomenological sense. We can say that it has a persistent, valenced, generalized, coordinated internal state that influences both its behavior and its physiology in ways consistent with the functional architecture of pain.

**Now the twist:** Suppose you run the same paradigm on a computer simulation of a crab, where you have programmed all five of the above behaviors in as rules. The simulation would produce behavior consistent with all five criteria. Does it have a functional emotion?

This is where Definition X shows its limits. The Anderson-Adolphs criteria describe *what the behavior looks like*; they do not specify *what mechanism produces the behavior*. The criteria were designed for biological nervous systems in which we have independent reasons to believe the behavior reflects a genuine internal state. Applied to a simulation, they would be satisfied, but the result would be trivially true by construction. The framework works as a *test for emotion in animals with nervous systems*; it is not a test for emotion in general.

## 5.6 The Gap That Does Not Close

The two large formal declarations of recent years on animal consciousness are worth knowing, both for what they claim and for the care with which they phrase those claims.

The *Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness* (2012) was signed by a prominent group of neuroscientists at a conference honoring Stephen Hawking. It stated that nonhuman animals "possess the neurological substrates that generate consciousness" — a sweeping claim that was widely reported as declaring that animals are conscious. What it actually said, if you read it closely, was that animals have the *structural prerequisites* for consciousness, not that consciousness itself has been demonstrated.

The *New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness* (2024), organized by philosophers and scientists at NYU, was more careful. It stated: "There is strong scientific support for attributions of conscious experience to other mammals and to birds. The empirical evidence indicates that this is also the case for many other vertebrates." Then it added: "Regarding invertebrates, we are more cautious. We note that the empirical evidence for conscious experience in invertebrates, including insects, is more limited." But it concluded: "We believe that, for some invertebrates, such as insects, decapod crustaceans, and cephalopod molluscs, there is at least a realistic possibility of conscious experience."

*Realistic possibility* is doing real work in that sentence. It is not claiming that bees feel pessimism the way you feel anxious. It is claiming that the probability is high enough that ignoring it would be a moral error. The practical implication: we cannot afford to wait for certainty before building it into how we treat these animals.

I think this is the right standard. The gap between Definition X and Definition Y may never close — not because science will fail to improve, but because Definition Y is asking a question that third-person methods may be structurally unable to answer. There may be no observation from outside a nervous system that tells you whether there is something it is like to be inside it. This is the hard problem of consciousness, and it is hard in the specific sense that no amount of functional evidence resolves it.

What the evidence *can* do is make Definition Y progressively less plausible to deny. The pattern of evidence — a single neuromodulatory architecture conserved from worms to vertebrates, functional states meeting all five Anderson-Adolphs criteria, cognitive-bias results that hold across phylogenetically distant animals, integration centers that bind sensory and affective information in insects just as they do in mammals — is consistent with the hypothesis that *something* runs through the whole nervous system continuum. The hypothesis that there is a hard cutoff, above which there is experience and below which there is nothing, has no mechanism. It is a stipulation, not a theory.

This is where my own view sits. I do not know whether the shaken bee felt anxious. I know that it had a state that did to its cognition what anxiety does to ours. That may not be the same thing. But it is not nothing.

## 5.7 Why the Bee Is the Right Place to Have This Argument

I want to close the empirical section by defending the choice of focus.

The bee is small. Its brain is small. The behavior is, stripped to its essence, not breathtaking. A biased interpretation of an ambiguous odor — this does not have the drama of an elephant grieving, or a dog recognizing its owner's face, or a chimpanzee using a tool. Why make so much of it?

Because the bee is precisely the case where the definitions come apart most cleanly. If we were discussing human anger, Definitions X and Y would both apply and most of the interesting questions would be about degree, context, and phenomenological texture. If we were discussing a robot that displays angry-looking facial expressions, neither definition would apply, and the question would be settled trivially.

The bee sits in the gap. The functional evidence is solid enough that dismissing it requires you to explicitly reject the Anderson-Adolphs criteria or to argue that the criteria do not generalize to insects, and to give a principled reason why. The phenomenological evidence is weak enough that asserting subjective experience requires you to commit to a position you cannot test. The bee forces the choice between definitions into the open, where it can be examined rather than assumed.

The same argument applies with increasing force as we move up the animal phylogeny. By the time we reach dogs (Chapter 10) and primates (Chapters 10 and 11), the case for Definition Y is harder to dismiss, and the phenomenological gap that looks so wide at the bee starts to look narrower. But the argument's structure is the same at every level. What changes is the weight of the evidence.

Every chapter in Part Two of this book is, among other things, an exercise in this choice of definition. The reader who wants to claim that only humans have *real* emotions — emotions in the full Definition Y sense — is entitled to that position. But they have to be prepared to say exactly where on the phylogenetic ladder the capacity appears and what mechanism produces it at that point and not before. The ladder has no rungs that are not connected to the rungs below them. The mechanism is conserved. The function is conserved. The claim that the phenomenology appears suddenly, at some arbitrary point, without a conserved mechanism, is the claim that needs defending.

The bee makes that claim difficult to hold without noticing that you are holding it.

## Chapter Summary

**What you can do now that you could not before this chapter:**

The central skill this chapter adds is the ability to evaluate a claim about animal emotion without collapsing Definition X and Definition Y into each other. These are different questions. They have different methods. They have different answers. Getting them right requires holding both at once.

Under Definition X — emotion as functional state meeting the Anderson-Adolphs criteria — the evidence establishes that at least some invertebrates have emotional states. Bees, several crustacean species, and some insect preparations meet the criteria. The mechanism, in the bee case, involves dopamine in a way that is recognizably related to dopamine's role in vertebrate affect.

Under Definition Y — emotion as subjectively experienced feeling — the evidence does not establish this for any invertebrate, and may not be able to. The New York Declaration's language is the right standard: *realistic possibility*, not certainty.

**The one idea that matters most:** The cognitive-bias paradigm is how we test for valenced internal states without requiring the animal to report its experience. It works by inducing a known affective state and measuring whether interpretation of ambiguous information tilts in the predicted direction. When it works across pharmacological manipulations that identify the neuromodulator involved, it provides the strongest available evidence for functional emotion.

**The common mistake to watch for:** Asserting that the Anderson-Adolphs criteria being met means the animal *feels* the state. The criteria describe functional architecture, not phenomenology. A system could meet all five criteria without having any phenomenology at all (the headless cockroach case is the extreme illustration). The criteria are necessary evidence for emotional capacity; they are not sufficient evidence for conscious experience.

**The Feynman test:** Can you explain, in plain language, why a bee that has received a surprising drop of sucrose subsequently approaches an ambiguous color cylinder faster than a control bee, *even though* the ambiguous cylinder has nothing to do with the sucrose — and why blocking dopamine before the sucrose eliminates this effect? If you can, you understand the Perry experiment. If not, reread §5.3.

## Exercises

**Warm-up: Direct Application**

1. A study finds that starlings housed in barren cages without enrichment subsequently interpret ambiguous sounds as more likely to be predator calls than starlings housed in enriched environments. Apply the Anderson-Adolphs criteria to this result. Which criteria are clearly met by this finding? Which criteria would require additional experiments to evaluate?

2. In the Perry (2016) bumblebee experiment, why was it important that the ambiguous test cylinder was a *color the bees had never seen before*, rather than a color somewhere between the two trained colors? What confound would have been introduced if the test stimulus were a familiar, partially-trained color?

3. The text distinguishes between *mood* and *appetitive motivation* as two possible explanations for the optimistic bias in bumblebees. Design one experiment that could distinguish between these two interpretations. Specify what result would support the mood interpretation and what result would support the motivation interpretation.

**Application: Translation**

4. A veterinary researcher proposes that pigs in intensive confinement systems show "emotional dysregulation" as evidenced by: (a) stereotypic behaviors (repetitive movements with no obvious function) that persist for hours after the stressful episode ends, (b) increased aggression toward pen-mates that scales with the duration of confinement, (c) biased interpretation of ambiguous stimuli toward negative outcomes in a cognitive-bias assay, and (d) simultaneous elevations of cortisol and reduced exploratory behavior. Evaluate this claim against the Anderson-Adolphs criteria. Are all five criteria met? Which one has the weakest evidence in this summary?

5. Consider a machine-learning model trained for sentiment analysis — it reads a text and reports "positive," "neutral," or "negative" affect. Compare this system to a bee in the cognitive-bias paradigm. In what sense does the model have something like Definition X emotion? In what sense does it not? What would the model need to have that it currently lacks for the Anderson-Adolphs criteria to be satisfied?

6. The New York Declaration uses the phrase "realistic possibility of conscious experience" rather than "has conscious experience." Why is this phrasing more scientifically defensible? What would it take — what kind of evidence — to move from "realistic possibility" to "strong evidence for"? Do you think that evidence could, in principle, exist?

**Synthesis: Combining Concepts**

7. Chapter 3 described how neuromodulators (dopamine and serotonin in *C. elegans*) act as slow broadcast signals that re-weight sensory integration based on internal state. Chapter 5 shows that blocking dopamine in bumblebees eliminates an affective bias. How do these two findings together support — or challenge — the claim that emotion in its functional sense is a *scaled-up version of neuromodulatory state management* rather than a qualitatively new capacity?

8. The headless cockroach result shows that a learned-helpless-like state can be implemented in a thoracic ganglion with no brain. The bee's optimistic bias requires the mushroom bodies, a higher integration center. What does this difference suggest about the relationship between the complexity of the neural substrate and the richness of the functional state? Is there reason to think that a state running through a higher integration center is *more* of an emotion, or just differently organized?

9. Throughout this chapter, the evidence for functional emotion in invertebrates has been built from: (1) behavioral paradigms (cognitive bias, learned helplessness), (2) pharmacological manipulations (dopamine blockade), and (3) neural substrate identification (mushroom bodies). Construct an argument that these three lines of evidence, combined, provide stronger support for the claim than any one alone. What would be missing if you had only behavioral data, only pharmacological data, or only neural substrate data?

**Challenge: Pushing Forward**

10. *(Open-ended)* I argued in §5.6 that Definition Y — emotion as subjectively experienced feeling — may be permanently unresolvable by third-person methods, even in principle. Some philosophers and neuroscientists disagree: they argue that certain neural correlates of consciousness (integrated information, higher-order representations, global workspace broadcast) provide objective markers that can be measured from outside. If one of these theories is correct, how would it change the conclusion of this chapter? What specifically would we need to measure in the bee, and what result would shift the probability of Definition Y being satisfied? Begin developing your view on which (if any) theory of consciousness is most likely to provide this kind of evidence.

## Connections Forward

Chapter 5 established that internal states with all the functional hallmarks of emotion appear in invertebrates, tied to a conserved neuromodulatory architecture. The next two chapters trace what happens when that architecture encounters richer sensory environments.

**Chapter 6** (*Pattern Recognition and Perception*) takes up the fish — animals with the first true cortex-like structures — and asks how a nervous system goes from labeling stimuli (labeled lines in Chapter 3) to *recognizing objects across contexts* (the invariance problem). The affective coloring of pattern recognition — some patterns are scary, some are appetitive — will connect back to the neuromodulatory state machinery developed here.

**Chapter 10** (*Social and Emotional Intelligence*) returns to emotion with much higher-powered evidence. When we get to dogs and primates, the phenomenological gap that looks so wide at the bee starts to narrow in ways that are harder to dismiss. The framework you built in this chapter — especially the Anderson-Adolphs criteria and the cognitive-bias paradigm — is the lens you will use to evaluate those cases.

The question this chapter leaves open: if emotion as functional state is a graded continuum from worms to humans, what does *richness* of emotional experience mean? The bee's optimistic bias and your feeling of excitement at unexpected good news seem qualitatively different. Are they? What would a theory of emotional richness need to specify to distinguish them at the architectural level? Hold the question. We will return to it.

---

*The shaken bee did not choose her pessimism. She did not deliberate. She did not decide that, given recent events, the world looked more dangerous than it had. The state arose from chemistry, and the chemistry changed what everything else meant. This is true of her. It is also true of us. The question is not whether that makes her less — it is whether it makes us different in kind, or only in degree.*

**Chapter Notes and Primary Sources**

The shaken-bee cognitive-bias study was published by Bateson, Desire, Gartside, and Wright in *Current Biology* in 2011 and remains the foundational invertebrate application of the cognitive-bias paradigm.

The dopamine-blockade result in bumblebees was published by Perry, Baciadonna, and Chittka in *Science* in 2016. The appetitive motivation critique is associated principally with Martin Giurfa's lab; the specific predictions that would distinguish mood from motivation are articulated clearly in Chittka's response to those critiques in subsequent correspondence.

The Anderson-Adolphs five-criteria framework appeared in *Cell* in 2014. The cognitive-bias paradigm was formalized by Mendl, Burman, and Paul in *Proceedings of the Royal Society B* in 2010.

Robert Elwood's crustacean pain research spans multiple papers from 2009 to the present; the hermit-crab shell-value trade-off study was published in *Animal Behaviour* in 2009. The Birch et al. review on decapod sentience that informed the Animal Welfare (Sentience) Act 2022 was published as a UK government-commissioned report in 2021.

The Horridge (1962) headless-cockroach learning result appeared in *Proceedings of the Royal Society B*. Subsequent learned-helplessness work with thoracic preparations has been published in various behavioral neuroscience journals through the 1990s and 2000s.

The Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness (2012) was drafted by Philip Low and signed at the Francis Crick Memorial Conference. The New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness (2024) was organized by Jeff Sebo and colleagues at NYU's Center for Environmental and Animal Protection.

Lisa Feldman Barrett's critique of basic emotion theory and facial expression classification systems appears in her 2017 book *How Emotions Are Made: The Secret Life of the Brain* and in subsequent papers including her 2019 work on the failure of AI affect-recognition systems in deployment.
