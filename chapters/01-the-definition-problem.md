# Chapter 1: The Definition Problem
## *Twenty-Four Theorists, Twenty-Four Definitions*

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

1. **Identify** at least six major competing definitions of intelligence and articulate what each captures and what it leaves out
2. **Explain** the Legg-Hutter formal definition and describe why it is selected as the book's working instrument
3. **Apply** Cosma Shalizi's statistical critique to explain why the *g* factor does not straightforwardly establish a single underlying mental ability
4. **Describe** Frans de Waal's *Umwelt* problem and demonstrate its implications for cross-species intelligence research
5. **Construct** an argument for why refusing to commit to a definition is not epistemically neutral
6. **Distinguish** between using a definition as an instrument versus claiming it as a final truth

**Prerequisites:** None. This is the book's first chapter. If you have strong intuitions about what intelligence *is*, this chapter will make them complicated. That is its purpose.

---

## Why This Chapter Matters

Every subsequent chapter in this book depends on one decision made here: which definition of intelligence we are using. Without that decision, comparisons across species are noise. With it, they become the same question asked twenty different ways.

But making that decision honestly requires first understanding why it is hard. The difficulty is not sloppiness. It is not that researchers have failed to think carefully. It is that *intelligence* may be the kind of word that picks out a family of related things rather than a single thing — and that different researchers, asking different questions about different organisms for different purposes, have reasonably converged on different members of that family.

This chapter surveys the major definitions, names their costs, and then commits to one — not as truth, but as instrument.

---

## 1.1 The Cold Open: An Honest Experiment, Twice

It is 1986, and two dozen of the most serious researchers in the field have been given the same task: write down what you mean by the word.

The request sounds trivial. It turns out to be anything but.

Robert Sternberg and Douglas Detterman had convened a symposium with what looked like a modest agenda — ask prominent theorists of intelligence to each define their subject. The resulting collection, *What Is Intelligence? Contemporary Viewpoints on Its Nature and Definition*, produced twenty-four definitions from twenty-four researchers. The American Psychological Association's subsequent task force, chaired by Ulric Neisser, would later summarize the situation in a single dry sentence:

> "When two dozen prominent theorists were recently asked to define intelligence, they gave two dozen, somewhat different, definitions."

Not roughly the same definition with minor variations. Twenty-four definitions that did not fully fit together, and in some cases barely seemed to address the same subject.

Now consider what happened the same year those proceedings were being compiled into print. In December 1994, fifty-two researchers signed an open letter in the *Wall Street Journal* titled *Mainstream Science on Intelligence*. The letter offered twenty-five numbered statements presented as the scientific consensus — that intelligence is a general mental capability, that it can be measured accurately, that it predicts outcomes across life domains. The letter was confident, precise, and broadly endorsed.

Two documents. Both careful. Both signed by serious researchers. One describing irresolvable definitional disagreement; the other describing settled consensus. Produced by overlapping communities. Published within a few years of each other.

This is the situation a book on intelligence has to start in.

Not because the researchers are sloppy. Not because the question is meaningless. But because intelligence is not the kind of thing that has had its definition sorted out yet. It is more like *life* in the middle of the nineteenth century: clearly a real distinction, demonstrably present in some systems and absent in others, and yet resisting every attempt to draw a clean boundary that includes everything we want and excludes everything we don't.

Crystals grow. Fires consume oxygen and reproduce themselves outward. Viruses copy themselves but cannot metabolize. The biologists of 1850 were not embarrassed by their failure to produce a single definition of life, because they understood the work was figuring out what the word *ought* to mean once you had looked carefully at a great many things that might or might not deserve it.

That is the temper of this book. The question mark in the title is there for a reason.

This chapter does not solve the definition problem. It surveys the major candidates, names what each gets right and what each leaves out, and then commits to one — not as the truth about intelligence, but as a working instrument precise enough to let us ask consistent questions across the full sweep of life, from nematode to language model.

## 1.2 The Candidates

The history of intelligence research is a history of researchers solving real problems and then discovering that their solutions applied only to the problems they were solving. Each of the following definitions was built for a purpose. Each captures something genuine. Each leaves something out.

### Binet (1905): Practical Judgment

Alfred Binet was a clinician, working in Paris at the turn of the twentieth century on a practical problem: which French children needed extra educational support? His 1905 paper described intelligence not as a substance to be measured but as a quality of action — *judgment*, *good sense*, *initiative*, *the faculty of adapting oneself to circumstances*.

Last on his list, and treated as essential, was *auto-critique*: the capacity to examine and correct one's own thinking.

Binet was also explicit about what he was not doing. He wrote against the idea that intelligence could be reduced to a single permanent number. He proposed *mental orthopedics* — targeted exercises to strengthen the capacities he was measuring — because he believed they were strengthable. His position was lost almost immediately. Lewis Terman brought the test to Stanford, the IQ movement turned the score into a fixed property of the child, and Binet's nuance evaporated into the culture.

**What this definition captures:** Intelligence as something visible only in the doing — in responding to a real situation, in the willingness to revise. It is action-first, not capacity-first.

**What it leaves out:** Any way of measuring the thing across organisms whose situations and timescales differ radically from ours. *Auto-critique* is hard to demonstrate in a worm. It is also hard to demonstrate in many fluent adults.

---

### Wechsler (1944): Aggregate Adaptive Capacity

David Wechsler, whose tests would become the most widely used in clinical practice, defined intelligence in *The Measurement of Adult Intelligence* as *the aggregate or global capacity of the individual to act purposefully, to think rationally, and to deal effectively with his environment*.

The word *aggregate* was doing real work. Wechsler understood intelligence as an effect — what happens when cognitive abilities, personality, and motivation combine in a real person dealing with a real world. His scales introduced *deviation IQ*, replacing Binet's ratio score with a standard position on a normal distribution. The verbal/performance split in his tests aimed at a richer profile of what a person could actually do.

**What this definition captures:** The integrative, whole-agent quality of intelligence. It refuses to reduce a person to a single cognitive module.

**What it leaves out:** Any way of saying what counts as *effective dealing* in terms that translate from a human in a city to a wasp in a meadow. Wechsler did not have wasps in mind. His definition implicitly centers the human ecological niche as the standard.

---

### Gardner (1983): Multiple Intelligences

Howard Gardner, in *Frames of Mind*, proposed not so much a definition as a refusal of one. Intelligence is not a unitary ability but a set of *biopsychological potentials* — linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, and later naturalist — each developmentally and neurally distinct.

He set out eight criteria for admitting a candidate to the list: dissociability under brain damage, savant existence, an identifiable core operation, a developmental trajectory, evolutionary plausibility, support from cognitive psychology, support from psychometrics, and amenability to a symbolic notation. The framework has been embraced in education and criticized sharply in psychometrics, where the seven or eight intelligences turn out to be substantially positively correlated with each other — providing, critics argue, evidence for the very *g* factor Gardner sought to displace.

**What this definition captures:** The obvious fact that human ability is uneven. Someone can be extraordinary at music and helpless at arithmetic. A single number erases this.

**What it leaves out:** An account of why we group these capacities under a single word, or how to test for an intelligence we have not yet named, or what the framework does when applied to species with entirely different cognitive profiles.

---

### Sternberg (1985): Triarchic

Robert Sternberg proposed three intelligences working together: *analytical* (the kind tested in school), *creative* (adapting to novelty), *practical* (the street intelligence that gets someone through a real problem). His distinctive move was *shaping* — intelligent agents do not merely adapt to environments, they actively modify them.

That move connects human innovation to the biological literature on niche construction: beavers build dams, earthworms turn soil, and the environment becomes part of the organism's cognitive apparatus. Intelligence, in this account, is partly a relationship between an agent and a world the agent is partly building.

---

### Legg and Hutter (2007): Goal Achievement Across Environments

Shane Legg and Marcus Hutter surveyed more than seventy serious definitions and distilled their shared content into a single sentence. Then they formalized it mathematically:

> **Intelligence measures an agent's ability to achieve goals in a wide range of environments.**

The mathematical version is a measure $\Upsilon$ over an agent $\pi$:

$$\Upsilon(\pi) = \sum_{\mu \in E} 2^{-K(\mu)} V^\pi_\mu$$

Read it slowly. $E$ is the set of all computable environments. $V^\pi_\mu$ is the agent's expected reward in environment $\mu$. The crucial term is $2^{-K(\mu)}$: here $K(\mu)$ is the *Kolmogorov complexity* of the environment — the length of the shortest program that fully describes it — and the negative exponent means simpler environments count more. The whole sum is Occam's Razor dressed in mathematics. An agent is intelligent to the degree that it does well across environments, weighted toward environments that have structure worth exploiting.

**What this definition captures:** A graded, operational, substrate-neutral property. It applies without modification to bacteria, bees, dogs, chess engines, and language models. There is no mention of neurons, no mention of consciousness, no mention of language. The measure is indifferent to what kind of thing you are.

**What it leaves out:** Consciousness, feeling, interior cognition. By this definition a sufficiently sophisticated thermostat has a small but non-zero amount of intelligence. The measure is also provably incomputable — you cannot evaluate the sum across infinitely many environments, and the answer depends on which Universal Turing Machine you choose to define complexity. It is a theoretical idealization; the practical test is always approximate.

---

### Wissner-Gross and Freer (2013): Entropy Maximization

Alexander Wissner-Gross and Cameron Freer proposed intelligence as a force that acts to *maximize future freedom of action*. Their equation for the *causal entropic force* is:

$$F = T \nabla S$$

Here $S$ is the *causal entropy* — a path-integral measure of the number of futures accessible from a given state — and $T$ is a temperature-like parameter. The system behaves *as if* pushed toward states from which more histories remain possible. Using only this principle, the authors produced simulated systems that spontaneously exhibited what looked like upright walking, tool use, and rudimentary social cooperation.

There is something there. The principle is real and connects interestingly to biological behavior. But organisms frequently *narrow* their futures — committing to a mate, defending a territory, charging a predator — for reasons that look more like goal pursuit than option preservation. The framework is elegant on toy problems and unproven on the full ecological stage.

---

### Chollet (2019): Skill-Acquisition Efficiency

François Chollet, working from within AI research, offered a definition aimed specifically at diagnosing what current AI systems lack: *intelligence is skill-acquisition efficiency over a scope of tasks, with respect to priors, experience, and generalization difficulty*.

The move is to stop measuring how good a system is at a specific task — that is mostly a measure of training data — and start measuring how quickly and broadly it learns to do something it has not seen before. Chollet operationalized this in the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC), a battery of small visual puzzles built on core human priors where each task supplies only a few input-output examples and the system must infer the rule.

ARC turns out to be easy for humans and very hard for systems trained on internet-scale data. The failure is illuminating: enormous banks of accumulated knowledge do not transfer to learning a new rule from scratch.

---

There are more. Cattell-Horn-Carroll's hierarchical model. Hofstadter's fluid analogies. Pearl's causal-reasoning hierarchy. Each does real work somewhere. None of them, examined honestly, settles the question.

## 1.3 Two Critiques That Cut Across Everything

Two arguments deserve their own treatment because they apply not just to one definition but to the entire enterprise of measuring intelligence. Before committing to any framework, we need to look at these honestly.

### Shalizi: The *g* Factor as Statistical Artifact

The statistician Cosma Shalizi, in an essay called *g, a Statistical Myth*, made an argument that should give any reader of intelligence research real pause. The claim is technical, but the point is plain.

Spearman's *g* — the general factor that emerges when you do principal component analysis on a battery of cognitive test scores — is sometimes treated as a discovered property of the mind, a real underlying mental capacity that these tests are revealing. Shalizi's argument is that *any* set of positively correlated variables, regardless of what they measure, will yield a first principal component that loads positively on all of them. The mathematics forces it.

So finding a *g* in cognitive tests does not, by itself, demonstrate that there is one underlying mental ability. It demonstrates that the tests are positively correlated — which is also what you would find if intelligence were the joint output of many independent abilities that happen to share a common developmental or environmental bottleneck.

Take any three positively correlated measurements. Horsepower, top speed, and sticker price of cars. Run a principal component analysis. A "general car-quality factor" pops out of the analysis. Nobody thinks there is a single thing called car-quality under the hood. The math creates the appearance of it regardless.

The critique is not that *g* is useless — it predicts academic performance better than any single test alone. The argument is that interpreting *g* as a unified biological entity has run far ahead of the evidence. Defenders of *g* respond that the convergence across populations and across types of tests is too strong to be merely algebraic, and that biological correlates have been found. The argument continues.

For our purposes, what matters is this: **one of the most prestigious empirical results in intelligence research is contested at the level of its basic interpretation**, by serious people who agree on the data. This should make us modest.

### De Waal: The *Umwelt* Problem

The primatologist Frans de Waal, in *Are We Smart Enough to Know How Smart Animals Are?*, made a different cut. His target was the long history of testing animal intelligence by giving animals human-style tasks.

Chimpanzees were judged unable to use tools when nobody had watched them in the wild long enough. Octopuses were judged cognitively limited because they would not solve problems set in dim laboratory light at the wrong temperature. Dogs were tested on whether they would imitate humans by watching humans rather than other dogs. Each time, the animal failed the human test. Each time, the conclusion drawn was that the animal lacked the capacity — rather than that the test had been built around a human's *Umwelt*.

That word — *Umwelt* — comes from the German biologist Jakob von Uexküll, who used it in 1909 to describe the species-specific perceptual and behavioral world that is the proper object of study for any ethologist who wants to understand a creature on its own terms. A tick's *Umwelt* centers on temperature, butyric acid, and the feel of hair. A bat's *Umwelt* is a world of echoes. A dog's *Umwelt* is organized around smell with a richness we can barely imagine. Testing any of these organisms on a human's terms tells you about the human test, not about the organism.

De Waal's point is not that animals are smarter than we thought — sometimes they are, sometimes they are not. The point is that you cannot know until you have built the test inside the animal's *Umwelt*, and that the history of comparative cognition is partly the history of slowly figuring this out.

Both critiques apply to every definition in the survey above. Shalizi cuts at the data infrastructure on which psychometric definitions rest. De Waal cuts at the assumption that there is one place to stand from which to evaluate intelligence at all.

## 1.4 The Choice Is Not Neutral

Here is the thing about definitions that is often missed: a definition is not just a label. It is a sieve. Whichever you pick determines what falls through and what stays.

Pick Binet's *judgment*. A worm cannot be intelligent. Nematodes do not exercise judgment; they follow chemical gradients via a fixed neural rule of considerable elegance. Whatever they are doing, it is not what Binet meant.

Pick Wechsler's *aggregate adaptive capacity*. The worm starts to slip through. Worms habituate, learn associatively, imprint on smells and temperatures. They modulate behavior based on experience. Wechsler did not write his definition with worms in mind, but the definition does not exclude them.

Pick Gardner. The worm has none of the eight intelligences in any recognizable form. But a chess engine has logical-mathematical intelligence in abundance and nothing else. A border collie has interpersonal intelligence — Chaser, the collie studied by Pilley and Reid, learned 1,022 proper-noun referents and inferred new ones by exclusion — and modest spatial intelligence. The shape of the answer depends entirely on which capacity you ask about.

Pick Legg and Hutter. Now everything is on the table. The worm is intelligent in a modest way. The collie more so. You enormously so. A chess engine is highly intelligent in one environment and absent everywhere else. A bacterium is intelligent in the chemical-gradient sense in which it earns its living.

Pick Wissner-Gross. The bacterium starts looking particularly interesting, because keeping options open is exactly what bacterial tumbling behavior does — and human territorial defense, by contrast, is a poor fit for the framework.

Pick Chollet. A nematode scores low: it learns slowly, from many examples, in a narrow range. A crow scores very high: novel rule, few examples, broad transfer. A current large language model scores low in a different way — vast skill, achieved through training on more text than any human will read in a lifetime, but poor transfer to novel rules from scratch.

The point is not that one of these sieves is the right one. The point is that the answer to "is X intelligent?" depends entirely on the definition you began with, and there is no neutral place to begin. Refusing to commit is not neutrality — it is leaving the choice unspecified, which means whichever definition floats into view at any given moment does the work, often invisibly.

Most heated arguments about whether AI is "really" intelligent collapse the moment you ask which definition each side is using. They are not usually disagreeing about empirical facts. They are using different sieves and not noticing.

So this book commits.

## 1.5 The Working Instrument

The definition this book uses, throughout, from nematodes to language models, is Legg and Hutter's:

> **Intelligence is an agent's ability to achieve goals across a wide range of environments.**

I am not adopting it because I think it is the final truth about intelligence. I am adopting it because it is the most useful instrument for the comparative work this book wants to do. Three reasons.

**First, it is operational.** Given a candidate agent and a candidate environment, you can run an experiment. Did the agent achieve the goal? How does success change as the environment varies? The definition gives empirical grip — even though the full formal version is incomputable, comparative versions are eminently testable. We can ask whether the nematode achieves its goals across chemical environments. We can ask whether the chess engine achieves its goals outside chess. We get different answers, and both answers are informative.

**Second, it is substrate-neutral.** It does not mention neurons, consciousness, or language. It can be applied without modification to a bacterium, a wasp, a fish, a dog, and a piece of software. Every other definition in the survey privileges some substrate or capacity that is shared only across part of the phylogenetic ladder. Binet's *auto-critique* assumes a self that can examine itself. Wechsler's *purposeful action* leans on the kind of purposes humans recognize. Gardner's seven intelligences are seven specifically human ways of being smart. Sternberg's three are calibrated to an agent that goes to school and gets a job. Only Legg-Hutter extends cleanly down to the most minimal cases.

**Third, it is graded.** There is no sharp threshold at which something becomes intelligent. The width of environments and the range of goals each varies continuously. A nematode, a chess engine, and a human all have non-zero intelligence by this definition; the interesting question is what *shape* their intelligence takes, not whether they have any.

The cost is real and worth naming explicitly. The Legg-Hutter definition flattens the affective and social texture of cognition. It treats agents as goal-pursuers when many real animals are also moods, memories, attachments, social positions — whole worlds of inner life that the reward signal framework cannot easily accommodate. The definition will struggle most in the chapters on emotion, social intelligence, and creativity. When it struggles, I will say so. In those places I will pull a different tool from the wall — Sternberg when discussing how niche construction shapes the environment that shapes the agent; Gardner when distinguishing musical from spatial cognition; Chollet when asking whether a language model is learning or retrieving; de Waal when the comparative test has been built around the wrong *Umwelt*; Pearl's hierarchy when asking what current AI can and cannot do causally.

A working instrument is allowed to be imperfect. It is not allowed to be hidden.

## 1.6 What Stays Open

Two things are worth being explicit about now before the comparative work begins.

First: under different definitions, the same organism will receive different verdicts, and both verdicts are informative. Under Wechsler, a slime mold is not intelligent. Under Legg-Hutter, a slime mold solving a maze — and we will meet one in Chapter 2 — is intelligent in the maze-solving environment, in a modest but unmistakable way. Holding both answers in mind without pretending one cancels the other is the move. The book does not tell you what bees "really" feel. It shows you what they do, what mechanisms produce that behavior, which definitions include them in the category of the intelligent and which exclude them, and where the current evidence is strong enough to settle the question and where it is not.

Second: the question mark in the title is the thesis. There is no claim made anywhere in this book that we have arrived at the final definition of intelligence. The claim is that we have a working instrument, that it lets us compare a worm and a crow and a language model on the same axis, and that the comparison is worth making even though the instrument will not survive every chapter unscathed.

The next chapter walks further back than most discussions of intelligence dare to go — to bacteria, slime molds, and plants — asking whether anything a brainless organism does deserves the word at all. The answer, again, depends on which sieve you started with. By the end of Chapter 2 you should believe that the choice we made here was real, unavoidable, and worth making.

The worm in the petri dish is waiting.

## 1.7 Deep Mechanism: What a Definition Actually Does

This section pauses the narrative to explain, from first principles, the epistemological function of a scientific definition — because understanding this is prerequisite to understanding why the definition debate in intelligence research is not a failure of the field but a reflection of something genuine about the subject.

### Definitions as Boundary-Drawing Devices

In everyday speech, we use words as labels for things we already recognize. "Table," "hunger," "red" — we learn these by ostension, by someone pointing at examples until the pattern clicks. Scientific definitions work differently. They are not labels for pre-identified things; they are decisions about how to draw a boundary through a continuum of cases.

Consider temperature. We experience warmth and cold before we have any formal concept of temperature. The scientific definition — average kinetic energy of particles — is not what "temperature" meant before; it is a decision to let *that* property be the one the word tracks, rather than the felt experience of warmth or the reading on a mercury column. The decision was made because that property let us build thermodynamics.

Intelligence works the same way. We experience some beings as intelligent and others as not, long before we have a definition. The scientific project is to choose a definition — a specific boundary-drawing decision — that lets us build something useful. Different purposes suggest different decisions.

### Why the Disagreement Persists

The disagreement about intelligence definitions has persisted for over a century because the researchers disagreeing are not simply confused. They are using *intelligence* to do different scientific work.

Binet needed a definition that identified children who would benefit from remedial instruction. A practical, action-based, auto-critical notion of judgment was the right tool for that purpose.

Spearman needed a definition that connected to measurable psychometric tests in a European adult population. A statistical factor emerging from test correlations served that purpose.

Legg and Hutter needed a definition that applied across all possible computational agents — including agents not yet built. A formal measure over environments was the right tool.

These are not competing answers to the same question. They are answers to different questions, all of which happen to use the word *intelligence*.

### What "Natural Kind" Would Mean

There is a philosophical concept that bears on this. A *natural kind* is a category that tracks a real division in nature — not merely a convenient grouping for human purposes, but a genuine fault line in the world's structure. Gold is a natural kind: all gold atoms have the same atomic number, and that atomic number determines their properties. Species are plausible natural kinds, if the biological species concept is right. Mass is a natural kind.

The question for intelligence is: is it a natural kind? Is there a single property, or tight cluster of properties, that all and only intelligent things share — such that any correct definition would converge on it?

There is reason to doubt this. The survey above suggests that what we call "intelligence" is better described as a *family resemblance* in Wittgenstein's sense: a cluster of related capacities (learning, planning, generalizing, communicating, tool use, self-modeling) that overlap in complex ways, with no single capacity shared by all members of the family. Humans have all of them in developed form. Bees have some robustly. Chess engines have one in superhuman form and none of the others. Bacteria have a minimal version of learning.

If intelligence is a family resemblance rather than a natural kind, then there is no single "correct" definition to find. Every definition captures some members of the family fully, others partially, and excludes others. The most we can do is choose a definition that is useful for our purposes — explicit about what it includes and excludes — and be honest about the costs.

This is what we are doing.

### Why Substrate-Neutrality Matters for This Book

One specific desideratum deserves explanation. Why does it matter so much that the definition not privilege neurons or carbon-based chemistry?

Because the book is making a comparative argument. If we define intelligence in terms of neurons, the question "do plants have intelligence?" becomes definitionally closed before we have looked. If we define it in terms of flexible behavior and environmental achievement, the question remains open — and we can then discover that plants achieve goals (getting water, responding to herbivory) in ways that do not look like anything we associate with minds, and that this discovery is informative rather than settled in advance by the vocabulary.

The same logic applies to AI. If we define intelligence as something only biological systems can have, we have answered the question before conducting any investigation. The Legg-Hutter framework keeps the question empirically alive.

## Summary

This chapter has established the intellectual context for everything that follows.

**What we learned:**

- The definition of intelligence has not been settled. Twenty-four serious researchers in 1986 gave twenty-four definitions. That disagreement reflects a genuine feature of the subject, not a failure of the researchers.

- The major competing definitions each capture something real and each leave something out. Binet emphasized practical judgment and auto-critique. Wechsler emphasized aggregate adaptive capacity. Gardner emphasized multiple distinct biopsychological potentials. Sternberg added shaping of environments. Legg and Hutter formalized goal achievement across environments. Chollet emphasized skill-acquisition efficiency.

- Two cross-cutting critiques apply to the entire enterprise. Shalizi's critique of *g* demonstrates that the most prestigious empirical result in intelligence research — a general factor underlying cognitive test performance — may reflect a mathematical artifact rather than a discovered biological unity. De Waal's *Umwelt* problem demonstrates that comparative cognition research is systematically biased toward human-centered task design, which systematically underestimates animal intelligence.

- The choice of definition is not neutral. Different definitions include and exclude different organisms. Refusing to choose simply allows an unexamined default to do the work invisibly.

- The working instrument for this book is Legg and Hutter's: *intelligence is an agent's ability to achieve goals across a wide range of environments*. It is chosen because it is operational, substrate-neutral, and graded. Its costs — flattening of affect, treatment of organisms as goal-pursuers — are real, acknowledged, and will be explicitly flagged when they become relevant.

- The question mark in the title is the thesis. We have a working instrument, not a final answer.

**The common mistake to watch for:**

Assuming that because you have found a definition that works well for humans, it must work equally well for other organisms. The most persistent error in comparative cognition research is building the test around the experimenter's *Umwelt* and then concluding from the animal's failure that the animal lacks the capacity.

**What you should now be able to teach someone else:**

Why the Legg-Hutter definition was chosen for this book, what its costs are, and why refusing to choose a definition is not a neutral option.

Chollet's skill-acquisition efficiency is the most interesting near-rival. If comparative cognition research continues to converge on learning rate across novel tasks as the cleanest cross-species measure, the instrument may need revision.*

*Still puzzling: I do not understand why the field has not converged. Twenty-four theorists in 1986. Fifty-two signing a confident statement in 1994. A cautious task force the same year. Seventy-plus definitions in Legg and Hutter's 2007 survey. The persistence of the disagreement suggests either that the researchers are interested in genuinely different things, all of them real, or that the term picks out a family resemblance rather than a natural kind. I lean toward the second reading. I am not yet sure.*
