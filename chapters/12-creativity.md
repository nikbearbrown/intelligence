# Chapter 12 — Creativity: Where the Evidence Gets Thin
*Individual innovation is widespread; the mechanism that turns it into culture is not.*

The aviary is temperature-controlled, quiet, and unremarkable in the way that research facilities are — beige walls, fluorescent overhead light, the faint mechanical hum of ventilation. None of that matters to the New Caledonian crow standing on a perch at the Max Planck Institute for Ornithology. What matters is the transparent box on the table in front of her, and the food inside it, and the slot in the side that is too narrow for her body and too short for any single one of the cylindrical shafts and hollow tubes lying on the table next to the box.

The crow's name is Mango.

She picks up a shaft. She picks up a tube. She fits the shaft into the tube, end to end. She uses the assembled stick — now long enough — to push the food out through the slot.

No training. No demonstration. No component of this behavior had ever been observed in any non-human animal outside the great-ape lineage. When Auguste von Bayern and her colleagues published the result in 2018, four of eight tested crows had produced compound tools spontaneously. Mango, working through a progressively harder version of the problem, had assembled a four-piece tool when the three-piece version was insufficient.

The result moved a boundary. Until 2018, compound tool manufacture had been one of a handful of behaviors cited as plausibly unique to apes. Mango moved it.

This is where the chapter has to be careful, because it is exactly here — at the moment of genuine surprise, when a crow does something we did not expect — that the machinery of anthropomorphic projection is most ready to run. Mango did something extraordinary. She also lives in a species that routinely manufactures standardized hook tools from pandanus leaves and forked branches in the wild, behaviors whose complexity had already challenged simple accounts of what only humans do. The compound-tool result is real. So is the context that makes it harder to call unprecedented than the headlines suggested. Both are part of the story.

That tension — between the genuine phenomenon and the genuine interpretive difficulty — is this chapter.

---

Before the evidence, the problem of definition. A creative act must satisfy three components. It must be novel — not in the agent's existing repertoire, not a routine variant of a familiar behavior. It must be useful — not random; it must solve or advance a problem. And it must be generated by something deserving the name intentionality — a directed cognitive act, not a side-effect of other processes.

Each of the three is harder to operationalize than it looks.

Novelty runs immediately into the question of new to whom. A behavior that is new to a particular individual might be old in the species (evolved predisposition), old in the population (cultural transmission), or old in the lineage (deep evolutionary preparation that has never been triggered before because the right stimulus never appeared). Most comparative research is studying psychological creativity — new to the individual producing it. Whether psychological creativity in a crow is the same kind of thing we mean when we call a human composer creative is a question I cannot answer here, but I can name it as open.

The Mango result sits exactly at this intersection. Her compound-tool assembly was new to her — she had not been trained to do it, had not seen it done. Whether it was new to New Caledonian crows as a species is less clear, because the species routinely manufactures complex single-component tools, and the cognitive operations required for compound assembly may not be as far from that repertoire as the result first appeared. Novelty, on inspection, is a graded property.

Utility runs into the post hoc problem. We observe that Mango retrieved the food. We conclude the behavior was useful. But we know it was useful only because we watched the outcome — which means our judgments about what counts as creative are downstream of outcome evaluation. A behavior that succeeded looks like creative problem-solving; the same behavior that failed looks like random exploration. The agent's cognitive process was, in both cases, identical. To rate creativity by outcome alone is to confuse what the agent is doing with what the world rewards.

Intentionality is the hardest of the three, and it is where the burden of inference is greatest. To say a behavior was creative we want to say something about the cognitive process that produced it — that the agent had a generative goal state, that the behavior was directed by attention rather than assembling itself by accident from stimulated reflexes. This is not directly observable from outside. We infer it from behavior, and the inference is exactly where anthropomorphic projection lives.

Two errors are available, not one. The first is to over-attribute: to see a surprising behavior and conclude that the agent was thinking about the result in a way that requires rich intentional states, when the behavior might have been produced by a simpler process. The second is to under-attribute: to refuse to credit intentionality to any non-human agent because we cannot directly observe their mental states, thereby setting an unfalsifiable standard that dismisses real evidence. Both errors have distorted the comparative-creativity literature, and both appear in the cases that follow.

There is a final distinction worth naming before the cases. Product-oriented approaches to studying creativity look at the artifact or behavior and ask whether it satisfies novelty and utility criteria. Process-oriented approaches ask what cognitive operations produced it. Most experiments in non-human creativity are, by necessity, product-oriented — cognitive process is hard to access in animals that cannot report their mental states. But most cognitive theories say creativity is ultimately a process, not a product: it is what the mind is doing, not just what comes out. The honest interpretation of any case in this chapter has to hold both sides in view.

---

New Caledonian crows, in the wild, manufacture two distinct types of hook tool: hooked twigs bent from forked branches, and stepped-cut barbed strips from pandanus leaves. The species-level case begins with Gavin Hunt's 1996 paper in *Nature*, which documented these behaviors and noted three features that distinguished them from most other animal tool use. The behaviors are standardized: different individuals in different populations produce recognizably similar shapes, suggesting learned templates rather than individual improvisation. They are diverse: multiple tool types in the same species. And they involve form imposition: the bird modifies raw material to a specific shape rather than using available objects as-is.

The von Bayern compound-tool result extends this but adds something the wild-population data cannot easily provide. The individual birds who assembled compound tools in the experiment were not applying a memorized cultural template — the compound-tool configuration had not been part of their prior experience. They were doing something closer to on-the-spot combination: representing the goal, representing the available materials, and generating a configuration that satisfied the goal. That is the cognitive claim the result supports. The manipulation sequences of successful crows, which the study tracked in full, showed systematic exploration of the assembly possibilities rather than random variation with a lucky endpoint. The crows that succeeded were working toward something.

In a dry-season savanna in southeastern Senegal, a young female chimpanzee — not among the dominant males — moves along a line of trees, selecting branches. She breaks one away, strips the side branches, strips the bark, and sharpens the tip with her teeth. She approaches a hollow in a tree trunk, inserts the tool, and withdraws a lesser bushbaby, stunned or dead.

Jill Pruetz and Paco Bertolani's 2007 paper in *Current Biology* reported this behavior across twenty-two distinct hunting attempts at the Fongoli site in Senegal. Three features carry weight. The behavior is multi-step and sequential: each modification step prepares for the next, and the sequence is consistent across individuals and attempts. The result is used in a specific motor pattern — probing, not pounding — suggesting the tool's design is coordinated with a specific use-context. And most suggestive for the creativity question: the behavior was observed most frequently in females and young males, the demographic with least direct physical access to prey. Pruetz and Bertolani proposed that spear hunting was, in part, a response to resource competition — an innovation that opened a foraging niche that status-mediated methods had closed. The behavior may have emerged under specific pressures that created a problem for which the existing behavioral repertoire was insufficient. That is the closest the non-human primatology literature has come to documenting individual-level creative problem-solving driven by something like need in a wild population.

Off North Sulawesi, a veined octopus — eight arms, roughly the size of a human fist — is doing something that looks, at first, like walking on stilts. It is moving across exposed sand, arms extended awkwardly beneath it, supporting the weight of a coconut-shell half held against its body. The walk is slow. The octopus is conspicuous. A flatfish shoots past; the octopus freezes. Then continues.

Julian Finn, Tom Tregenza, and Mark Norman's 2009 paper in *Current Biology* reported that the veined octopus collects discarded coconut-shell halves, transports them across open terrain, and assembles pairs of shells into portable shelters when cover is needed. The awkward transport walk makes the octopus slow and conspicuous at a moment when the shells offer no protection. The shells only become useful after the walk, when the octopus assembles them. The theoretical weight of the result rests on one inference: the octopus is incurring present costs for future benefit. It is carrying the tools before it needs them. That inference makes the behavior a candidate for prospective tool use — tool acquisition decoupled from immediate use — which had previously been documented reliably only in great apes and a small number of corvids. Finding it in a cephalopod, an invertebrate lineage whose last common ancestor with vertebrates predates the Cambrian, suggested that prospective planning is not a vertebrate invention. The cautious note: Finn and colleagues documented the behavior with video evidence across multiple individuals but could not observe the cognitive process. An alternative account, in which the transport behavior is a fixed-action pattern evolved for this specific context, cannot be ruled out entirely on behavioral grounds alone.

In northern Australia, a great bowerbird moves among the objects arranged in the court of his bower — an avenue of sticks with a court spread out in front, strewn with stones, shells, bones, and berries. He picks up a large stone near the bower entrance and carries it further back. He steps into the avenue, looks out toward the court, steps back out, moves another object.

John Endler and colleagues documented that male great bowerbirds arrange objects in the bower court in a graded size order: small objects close to the female's viewing position inside the avenue, large objects far away. The gradient produces a forced-perspective illusion — from the female's position, the visual angular size of the objects appears more uniform than it actually is. Several features of this result make it the strongest case in the literature for something like sustained aesthetic decision-making in a non-human animal. The size-distance gradient is consistent across individual males. It varies in quality among males, and quality correlates with mating success. When the researchers experimentally disrupted a male's gradient — shuffling the objects — the male restored it within days, suggesting he was evaluating the court against an internal standard rather than executing a fixed program once. The restoration implies ongoing evaluation against a goal state. Whether it deserves the word *aesthetic* depends on whether *aesthetic* means anything more than behavior shaped by evaluation against a visual standard. I use it provisionally, with that definition on the table.

The comparative-creativity literature has characteristic failure modes that appear regularly in popular accounts, and each has produced a cycle of overclaim, complication, and correction.

The most famous individual case in the literature — the New Caledonian crow Betty, who in 2002 was reported to have spontaneously bent a wire into a hook to retrieve food — has been substantially complicated by later work. Wild New Caledonian crows routinely use hook tools and engage in object-shaping behavior; Betty's wire-bending was striking but not as discontinuous from the species' ordinary repertoire as the original presentation suggested. The single-trial aha moment, on careful reading, was an individual variant on a lineage that had already evolved the relevant substrate. This is not a discrediting of Betty or the result. It is a correction of the framing — the implication that a crow had, in a single moment of insight, invented hook tools from scratch.

The general failure mode is single-trial results from captive animals who have extensive training histories with experimental paradigms. The animal has learned, over hundreds of trials, that manipulating objects in specific ways produces rewards. On the target trial, it produces a novel manipulation. How much of the apparent creativity is the cognitive operation we want to credit, and how much is successful generalization from a dense reinforcement history? The controls required to answer this question are rarely in place in the original report.

A related problem is that captive animals used in creativity experiments often have extensive training histories with the same experimenters, the same apparatus designs, and the same class of problems. Expectation leaks. The handler who has trained an animal for months on tool-use paradigms is not a blank surface when the creativity test begins. The animal is reading the handler, who is reading the animal, in an ongoing bidirectional exchange that no experimental protocol fully controls. This is a reason to weight wild-population field data more heavily than single-lab results, other things equal.

Then there is retrospective rationalization. The successful trial gets reported. The forty-nine unsuccessful trials that preceded it do not appear in the paper. The successful trial is described in terms that assume it was the result of insight rather than trial-and-error. The correction: score the entire exploratory sequence, not just the successful endpoint. Count attempts. Measure directedness of search. Ask whether the sequence has the structure of systematic problem-solving or random exploration with a successful resolution. When this level of behavioral data is collected — as it was in the von Bayern compound-tool study, which tracked the full sequence of manipulations each crow performed — it strengthens the inference substantially. The crows that succeeded in assembling compound tools did not simply happen onto the right configuration.

The deepest failure mode is the one the theory-of-mind framework from Chapter 11 trains us to see: the human capacity for theory of mind, applied to non-human animals, produces vivid and compelling inferences about the mental states of agents whose behavior surprises us. When a crow fits a shaft into a tube, we experience something — a sense that the crow understood what it was doing. That experience is evidence about our own cognitive systems. It is not, directly, evidence about the crow's.

The check is to ask, for any creative behavior in a non-human animal: what is the simplest cognitive process that could produce this behavior? Is there a stimulus-response chain, a fixed-action pattern, an associative learning history, that would output the same behavior without requiring the richer intentional states we are attributing? If there is, the richer attribution is not compelled. If there is not — if the behavior requires something like goal-state representation, template application, and evaluation — then the richer attribution is warranted.

The cases above survive this check reasonably well. The compound-tool assembly requires, at minimum, something like a representation of the goal, a representation of the available materials, and the generation of a configuration that satisfies the goal — because no stimulus-response chain over the animal's prior experience contained the compound-assembly step. The forced-perspective bower restoration requires, at minimum, something like a visual standard against which the court is evaluated, because the restoration is specific and consistent across experimental disruptions. These are not ironclad proofs. They are the honest inference from available evidence.

---

There is a pattern in the cases above that the chapter has been moving toward and should now name directly.

Each case demonstrates creativity at the individual level. A single agent, facing a specific problem, generates a novel behavior. The cognitive operations are real. A goal state is held in mind. Materials are manipulated against it. The result is evaluated.

What none of these cases demonstrates is *cumulative* creativity — the process by which innovations build on previous innovations across generations, each generation inheriting the previous generation's best solutions and extending them. The cultural ratchet that Michael Tomasello has argued is distinctively human. The click that, accumulated over thousands of generations, produced the wheel, the printing press, the internet, the experiment we are using to study the crow.

Wild New Caledonian crows have been tool-using long enough that their hook-tool designs are regionally standardized — a form of cultural transmission. But the designs have not demonstrably gotten more complex over documented time. There is no crow equivalent of Stone Age to Bronze Age to Iron Age progression, in which each technological generation starts where the last left off and pushes further. The Fongoli chimpanzee spear-makers have been using spears long enough for the behavior to be culturally transmitted within the group, but spear design has not progressively elaborated. The bowerbird males produce increasingly high-quality forced-perspective gradients with age and experience — an individual learning curve — but the bower design itself has not accumulated complexity across generations.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: individual creativity vs. cumulative creativity as two distinct loops — left loop: individual agent generates novel behavior → evaluates against goal → behavior may spread culturally → loop ends; right loop (the ratchet): individual innovation → faithful transmission → next generation inherits and extends → further innovation → loop continues and accumulates — arrows should show what the left loop lacks that the right loop has: faithful transmission of means, not just ends; pedagogical intent; cumulative inheritance — student should see that the difference is not in the innovation step but in what happens afterward] -->

This is the boundary that, on current evidence, holds. Individual creativity — yes, in a small set of lineages, clearly. Cumulative creativity — not yet demonstrated in any non-human animal, even those whose individual creativity is well-established.

The operational gap is instructive. Cumulative creativity requires not just innovation but faithful transmission with variation — each generation must receive the previous generation's best solutions accurately enough that the next innovation can build on them, rather than starting from scratch. It requires something like teaching, imitation of means rather than just ends, and the motivation to transmit accurately. The cognitive cluster required for the ratchet — theory of mind, language, pedagogy, and the disposition to treat cultural knowledge as cumulative — may be the thing that is actually distinctive about human cognition. Not individual creativity, which crows and octopuses and bowerbirds have, but the mechanism that turns individual creativity into cultural accumulation.

---

I want to be precise about what the comparative record establishes, and what it does not.

Creativity, by any operationalization that allows a comparative experiment, exists in a small set of non-human lineages — corvids, great apes, cephalopods, bowerbirds — at a level that is qualitatively above reflex and qualitatively below human cumulative culture. The operations are real. A goal state is held in mind. Materials are manipulated against it. The result is evaluated. These are not small things. They are the same operations that, in the human case, scaled up across culture and time, have produced everything humans have done that other animals have not.

What is not yet in evidence in any non-human lineage is the ratchet — the mechanism that turns individual creativity into unbounded cultural accumulation. That may be the actual uniqueness.

The crow named Mango assembled four pieces to make a tool. The human kindergartner who watches the same demonstration can draw a picture of it, name what each part is for, explain why three pieces were not long enough, and tell a friend how to do it tomorrow. That last sentence — *tell a friend how to do it tomorrow* — is the ratchet. The next two chapters try to explain where it comes from.

The chapter is called *Where the Evidence Gets Thin* not because the evidence is weak — it is not — but because the further we push into the question of what kind of creativity the crow or the octopus or the bowerbird actually has, the more the evidence thins out precisely at the joints where the interesting theoretical questions live. The product is easier to see than the process. The process is what the theory is about. That gap is not a failure of the researchers who produced this evidence. It is an honest description of where the field stands, and where the next generation of experiments will have to go.
