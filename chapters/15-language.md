# Chapter 15: Language
## *What the Pointing Finger Reveals*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

1. **Distinguish** between communication and language, between imperative and declarative reference, and between the emotional expression system and the language system — and explain why each distinction matters for interpreting animal communication research
2. **Identify** Hockett's key design features and apply them to evaluate candidate animal communication systems, including the waggle dance
3. **Trace** the human language acquisition curriculum — from proto-conversations through joint attention to declarative pointing to questions — and explain why each stage scaffolds the next
4. **Evaluate** the ape language research record honestly, including both what the best-trained subjects achieved and what they consistently failed to produce
5. **Explain** why Broca's area and *FOXP2* are not the language organ and the language gene, and describe what a more defensible account of language's neural and genetic substrate looks like
6. **Apply** the Legg-Hutter framework to assess where language positions the human agent relative to other species in the comparative intelligence hierarchy

**Prerequisites:** Chapter 10 (Theory of Mind), Chapter 11 (Metacognition), and Chapter 14 (Language precursors in apes and cetaceans). You should be familiar with the joint attention concept from Chapter 10 and with the distinction between model-based and model-free cognition from Chapter 9.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Every chapter in this book has pushed the boundary of what counts as intelligence outward: to bacteria navigating chemical gradients, to slime molds solving mazes, to bees that appear to experience something like pessimism. Each expansion was earned by evidence, and each forced a more careful definition of what we were measuring.

Language is where that expansion stops — or at least where it slows dramatically. Not because the evidence of sophisticated communication in other species is absent. It is not absent. But because, after half a century of intensive research asking whether any other species possesses language, the answer remains: not quite. Not yet. Close in some dimensions, far in others, missing something whose name the field is still working out.

This chapter is about what that something is. It turns out to be less a single capacity and more a developmental program — a sequence of social steps that human children run before they can walk, that builds the cognitive infrastructure on which words are placed. Understand the program, and you understand why the chimpanzee, with a brain area structurally homologous to Broca's, cannot do what an eighteen-month-old does without instruction.

You also understand, by the chapter's end, why language is the cognitive adaptation whose extension by technology has been the most consequential in our species' history. Writing, printing, the internet — not substitutes for language, but successive scaffoldings that expand the space across which language reaches. The leitmotif this book has been tracking, from the Roomba to GPS to the LLM, is displayed most starkly in language, because language was the first thing we built scaffolding for, and we have been building ever since.

## 15.1 The Cold Open: A Finger Pointing at Nothing in Particular

She is fourteen months old. She sees a dog in the park — a fat, waddling basset hound — and she turns immediately to her mother's face. Her arm comes up, finger extended, aimed at the dog. She holds the point and watches her mother's eyes track toward the dog, and then she watches her mother's face for confirmation. She is not asking for the dog. She does not want anything. She wants her mother to see what she sees.

The behavior has a name: declarative pointing. It shows up reliably in human infants between nine and fourteen months, and by eighteen months children are doing it dozens of times a day — pointing at airplanes, at moons, at pictures in books, at nothing more remarkable than a truck going past. The function is pure: to share attention. To make sure that two minds are attending to the same thing at the same time, and to know they are.

Now consider the chimpanzee. It shares 98.7% of its DNA with us. It has a region in its left frontal lobe anatomically homologous to our Broca's area. It has been raised, in research programs across three decades, in human households and human laboratories by people who spoke to it, gestured to it, and recorded its every communicative act. It points well. It points frequently. It points at the banana behind the glass, at the toy on the high shelf, at the door it wants opened. Imperative pointing — pointing to request, to direct, to control — is solidly in the chimpanzee's communicative repertoire.

In thirty years of careful observation by people specifically looking, no chimpanzee has been reliably documented to point at a thing because the thing is interesting and someone else might like to see it too.

That asymmetry — so small-seeming, a finger directed at a basset hound — is the chapter's subject. What is the chimpanzee missing? Not the brain area. Not the motor control. Not the social bond with its human caretakers. Something else. Something that an infant acquires before she has any words, something that appears in the window between nine and fourteen months, something that no amount of enriched rearing has reliably produced in any other primate. This chapter is about what it is and why it matters.

## 15.2 Three Things Wearing One Word

Before the mechanism, three distinctions that most conversations about animal language collapse — and that collapsing them guarantees confusion.

### Communication Is Not Language

Communication is information transfer that affects another's behavior. By this definition, the plant that releases defense chemicals through mycorrhizal networks is communicating. The vervet monkey that gives three distinct alarm calls for leopard, eagle, and snake is communicating. The bee that waggles in a figure-eight is communicating. The animal kingdom is saturated with communication. Language is a particular kind of communication system, with a particular cluster of structural properties, and the question of whether any non-human species has language is not the question of whether any non-human species communicates. It is a much narrower question.

Charles Hockett proposed thirteen design features in 1960 to mark the boundary. Most are widely shared across species. Four matter most for the hard cases: *displacement* (talking about things not present), *productivity* (generating novel utterances from finite parts), *cultural transmission* (learned rather than hardwired), and *duality of patterning* (meaningless sounds combine into meaningful units, which combine into utterances). Hockett's claim: human language is the only system in which all thirteen features co-occur. The half-century since has refined rather than overturned that claim.

Hauser, Chomsky, and Fitch pushed the demarcation further in a 2002 *Science* paper, distinguishing the language faculty in the *broad* sense — shared sensorimotor and conceptual systems — from the language faculty in the *narrow* sense, which they argued is essentially *recursion*: the ability to embed structures within structures of the same type, without limit. *The dog that the cat that the mouse that the cheese attracted chased bit ran away.* That sentence has no ceiling. The capacity to generate and parse it is, on the HCF account, what is uniquely human. Pinker and Jackendoff challenged this framing in 2005, arguing it pruned too much from the uniquely human column and left too little to evolve gradually. The recursion criterion has not been adopted without debate. But it remains the sharpest single test: does the system allow unbounded generativity, or does it plateau?

### Imperative Is Not Declarative

The distinction that matters most for the ape data is simpler than recursion. Imperative communication uses a signal to control another's behavior: *I want that, give me that, come here*. Declarative communication uses a signal to align another's attention with one's own: *look at that, see this, isn't that interesting*.

Apes do imperative. Apes do not, in natural behavior or under language training, reliably do spontaneous declarative reference.

The transition from imperative to declarative is a cognitive shift. To communicate declaratively, you must model what the receiver is attending to, have a goal of altering that attention, and want to alter it for its own sake — not to acquire anything, not to change any behavior, but because two minds should be attending to the same thing. This requires, at minimum, a theory of what the other mind is currently attending to. It requires the joint attention capacity developed in Chapter 10. And it requires something beyond joint attention: the motivation to *share* mental states as ends in themselves, not just as means to behavioral control.

The fourteen-month-old pointing at the basset hound is not using a sophisticated theory of mind. She does not understand that her mother has a separable stream of experience that could be oriented elsewhere. What she has is a precursor: the drive to co-attend, to make her experience of the world a shared experience. That drive, in its earliest form, predates full theory of mind and may be what makes full theory of mind possible.

### Emotional Expression Is Not Language

The third distinction is the one the popular neuroscience of language most often obscures. There are two distinct neural systems for vocal communication. The first is ancient: a subcortical emotional expression system, conserved across mammals, that produces laughter, crying, screaming, the involuntary cry of pain, the groan of exertion. It is automatic, difficult to suppress, not under full voluntary control. The second is newer: a neocortical language system, dependent on cortical-subcortical loops, that produces symbolic speech.

The independence is not theoretical. It is established by a clean clinical double dissociation.

Patients with Broca's aphasia — damage to the left inferior frontal gyrus — lose the ability to produce grammatical speech. They cannot articulate "The boy walked to school." They can, however, still laugh at a joke, cry at grief, and curse fluently at a dropped hammer. The emotional expression system is intact.

Patients with pseudobulbar affect — damage to the descending motor pathways from the cortex — lose control of involuntary emotional vocalization. They laugh and cry uncontrollably, at inappropriate times, regardless of their subjective emotional state. Their language is perfectly intact; they produce grammatical sentences describing their bewilderment at their own crying. The language system is intact.

The same vocal apparatus. Two different systems.

The implication runs directly against a story that sounds intuitive. Human language did not evolve from primate calls by gradual semantic enrichment. The call system and the language system are parallel, not serial. Looking at vervet alarm calls for the precursors of human syntax is looking in the wrong place. The precursors of human syntax are in the cortex, in the arcuate fasciculus, in the social developmental program examined below — not in the subcortical vocal output system that vervets, chimpanzees, and humans share.

## 15.3 The One Concept: The Curriculum That Builds the Speaker

Here is the mechanism that most accounts of language leave out.

Human children acquire language not by downloading a module but by running a developmental program. The program is a sequence — a curriculum — in which each stage scaffolds the next, and in which the child is not a passive recipient but an active participant who contributes to the scaffolding itself. No other species has been documented to run anything like it.

### Stage One: Proto-Conversations (Two to Four Months)

Before any word exists, before any symbol, there is the turn. At two to four months, infants and caregivers engage in proto-conversations: alternating vocalizations and facial expressions in a rhythmic, turn-taking exchange that carries no semantic content at all. The infant coos; the mother responds; the infant coos again; the mother responds. The floor switches. Eye contact gates the exchange.

What the infant is learning, before any word has been learned, is the structure of dyadic symbolic exchange: that communication is bidirectional, that there is a producer and a receiver and the roles alternate, that attention to the other's face is how you know whose turn it is.

Chimpanzee mothers do not engage in proto-conversations with their infants. The comparative literature has documented no systematic equivalent in any non-human primate. The behavior is, as far as the evidence goes, specific to our species.

### Stage Two: Joint Attention (Nine to Twelve Months)

At nine to twelve months, the dyad becomes a triad. The child and the caregiver now both attend to a *third thing* — an object, an event, a picture — while monitoring each other's attention to it. The child follows the caregiver's gaze. The child points and looks back to confirm the caregiver looked. The child plays give-and-take with objects, where each handover is also an exchange of attention. This is joint attention, and it is the cognitive substrate on which words are placed.

By twelve months, declarative pointing is well-established and first words have appeared. But notice the order: the joint attention infrastructure comes first, and words are placed into a social frame that already exists. The child does not learn words and then begin attending jointly. She attends jointly and then uses words to populate the joint-attention frame with labels.

### Stage Three: Questions (Eighteen to Twenty-Four Months)

By eighteen to twenty-four months, the child does something that has no parallel in the animal kingdom: she asks questions. Not performative questions, not learned request forms, but genuine information-seeking: *What's that? Why? Where did it go?*

The question is the active form of joint attention. It is not just sharing the adult's attention; it is recruiting the adult's knowledge to fill a gap in the child's own. To ask "Why?" the child must know that the adult has information she lacks, that the adult can transmit that information, and that information-transmission is a legitimate purpose of communication — not just a means to get things done, but an end in itself.

Ape-language subjects, even the most extensively trained, do not produce question forms at any reliable rate. The single most-cited individual exception is Alex, the African grey parrot trained for thirty years by Irene Pepperberg, who looked into a mirror and asked *What color?* — and received the answer *grey*. One individual. One trainer. One documented instance over decades of work. That such a case exists at all is striking. That it remains, after half a century of comparative language research, essentially unique tells us something about how rare this particular cognitive operation is outside our species.

### The Statistical Engine Underneath

Sitting underneath this social curriculum is a statistical learning capacity that the field has only begun to characterize.

In 1996, Jenny Saffran, Richard Aslin, and Elissa Newport exposed eight-month-old infants to two minutes of a continuous stream of nonsense syllables — no pauses, no intonation cues, just a flat stream of sound. The only cue to word boundaries was the transitional probability between adjacent syllables: within a word, the probability that one syllable followed another was 1.0; across word boundaries, it was 0.33. After two minutes of exposure, the infants discriminated high-probability strings from low-probability ones in a preferential-looking test. They had, in two minutes of passive listening, extracted the statistical structure of a miniature language they had never heard before.

Eight-month-olds. Two minutes. Discrimination at the boundary.

The child's brain is doing distributional statistics in the background, automatically, before she can sit up unsupported. What the curriculum provides is not raw learning capacity — the raw capacity is present from birth — but a social scaffolding that orients the child toward linguistic input and motivates her to parse it. The statistical engine runs on the fuel the curriculum provides.

## 15.4 The Ape Data, Honestly

The ape language research program is one of the most ethically intensive and empirically demanding enterprises in the history of comparative psychology. It has produced genuine findings. It has also been subject to motivated interpretation that the field has spent decades working through. What the data actually show, as of the best current assessment, is worth stating plainly.

Apes can master symbol-referent pairing for hundreds of items. The bonobo Kanzi, at the Language Research Center in Atlanta, learned to use approximately 350 lexigrams — abstract symbols on a keyboard — and understood a great deal of spoken English directed at him. He could follow novel instructions, respond appropriately to sentences he had not heard before, and distinguish syntactic differences in some cases.

What the best-trained apes have not produced, across all subjects and all studies: recursive syntax; spontaneous question-asking; reliable displacement beyond the immediate context; and the declarative reference that a fourteen-month-old human child produces without instruction, without a training program, and without any particular intelligence on the charts.

Herbert Terrace's Project Nim, the largest and most carefully analyzed ape-language study conducted, is the clearest evidence. Nim Chimpsky was raised in a sign-language environment for nearly four years and acquired around 125 signs, producing nearly 20,000 multi-sign utterances. Terrace's analysis showed: most utterances were prompted by the trainer's prior utterance; longer utterances did not contain more meaning but repeated high-frequency signs in varying order; and no reliable word-order regularity emerged that could be called grammar. The most generous re-analyses have not overturned the central findings.

The comparison to the human child is exact and damning. The human child at eighteen months has a smaller vocabulary than Nim had, and her utterances are shorter. But her utterances contain more information, they are not prompted by her caregiver's prior utterance at anything like the same rate, she is producing question forms, and she is on a trajectory that leads, by thirty-six months, to fully recursive grammar. Nim's trajectory led to a plateau.

What the ape lacks is not the neural machinery for symbol-referent learning — clearly present. It is the social-developmental curriculum that orients that machinery toward language-building. And that curriculum begins, as described above, with something no ape has reliably been observed to do: turning to share attention with another mind for the sake of sharing attention.

## 15.5 Broca's Area Is Not the Language Organ

The popular account runs like this: language is located in Broca's area (left inferior frontal gyrus) and Wernicke's area (left posterior superior temporal gyrus), connected by the arcuate fasciculus. Damage to Broca's produces non-fluent aphasia; damage to Wernicke's produces fluent aphasia with comprehension deficits. These regions expanded in human evolution and are smaller in non-human primates; this is why apes can't talk.

Every clause of that account is either wrong or substantially incomplete.

Broca's area exists in chimpanzees, bonobos, and gorillas. Cantalupo and Hopkins showed in 2001 that the chimpanzee homolog of Brodmann's area 44 is left-hemisphere asymmetric — just as it is in humans. The machine part is there, and it is already lateralized. What is different in human brains is not the presence of the area but its integration into a broader network — specifically, the dramatic expansion of the arcuate fasciculus, the white-matter tract connecting temporal and frontal language regions, which is substantially larger in humans than in any other primate. Language is not a module in a box. It is a network property that depends on the connectivity between regions.

The same point applies to genes. The *FOXP2* gene, popularized as "the language gene" after the discovery that a mutation in it causes the speech and language disorder in the KE family, encodes a transcription factor expressed in developing neurons of the basal ganglia and cerebellum. Chimpanzees have *FOXP2*. Mice have it. Songbirds have it, where it is involved in vocal-motor learning. The two amino-acid changes that distinguish the human *FOXP2* variant from the chimpanzee's likely fine-tune the developmental timing and dosage of the protein in the motor circuits that subserve articulation. The KE family shows what happens when one of those calibrations is broken — not that *FOXP2* alone builds language.

The language organ, if anything deserves that name, is the developmental program the human child runs in her first three years. The ingredients are: a richly connected cortical network (including but not limited to Broca's and Wernicke's areas); a functioning *FOXP2* and its developmental partners; the human vocal tract; and the social curriculum — proto-conversations, joint attention, declarative pointing, question-asking — that builds the cognitive infrastructure before words arrive. Remove any ingredient and the program fails. The program is not localized. It is not encoded in a gene. It is a process, extended across early childhood, that is what language actually is.

## 15.6 Two Bookends

### Nicaragua

In 1979, a school for deaf children was founded in Managua, Nicaragua — the first systematic deaf education in the country's history. The children who arrived had no common language. They had developed, in isolation with their hearing families, idiosyncratic home-sign systems that could not communicate across individuals. No model signed language was available to them. Teachers attempted oral Spanish instruction.

What happened over the following decades was documented by Ann Senghas and Marie Coppola. The children, thrown together, began to pool their home signs. Within the first cohort, a shared system emerged — still relatively sparse, without the grammatical complexity of a natural language. But as younger children arrived, the system changed. The children who arrived earliest in development, young enough to absorb the emerging system as primary linguistic input, did not merely learn what their older peers had assembled. They reorganized it. They introduced spatial agreement — using the signing space to indicate who did what to whom. They developed verb morphology. By the second cohort, the system had all the structural complexity of any natural human language, and it had gotten there without a teacher and without a model.

The children built a language because they could not not build one. The drive to grammaticize symbolic communication is, on the evidence of Nicaraguan Sign Language, a biological imperative as robust as walking.

### The Bee

Karl von Frisch spent decades establishing the meaning of the waggle dance. A returning forager, having found a rich food source, performs a figure-eight on the vertical honeycomb: the straight run of the figure-eight encodes the direction of the food relative to the sun, the duration of the waggle encodes the distance, and the vigor encodes the quality. Recruits who observe the dance fly to the encoded location. Riley and colleagues confirmed this in 2005 using harmonic radar to track recruited bees across open fields: they flew to where the dance said.

The waggle dance has displacement — the food is not present during the dance. It has arbitrary mapping — the duration of the waggle scales to distance in a way that is not iconic. It has a symbolic element that would have been difficult to predict from first principles.

What it does not have is productivity. The bee cannot use the dance to talk about anything other than food sources, nest sites, and water — a small, fixed inventory of referents. There is no recursion. There is no generativity. There is no displacement beyond the foraging domain. The bee cannot dance about a dance. It cannot describe last Tuesday's foraging. It cannot explain why the flower patch was disappointing.

The waggle dance is the most sophisticated non-human communication system we know of. It is also, on every criterion that distinguishes language from communication, not language.

## 15.7 What We Have, and What It Cost

By the Legg-Hutter framework, language is an extraordinary amplifier of goal-achieving capacity across environments. With language, an agent can communicate its goals to other agents, recruit their resources, transmit its strategies across time to agents who were not present, and build on the accumulated strategies of thousands of predecessors without reinventing them. The human who reads Newton stands, epistemically, in a position no individual genius could occupy alone. The gap between human and non-human intelligence, on the Legg-Hutter measure, is as large as it is partly because language multiplies the effective range of goals an agent can pursue and environments in which it can achieve them.

But language's multiplying effect does not stop at biological language. Writing externalizes memory and lets the dead speak to the living. Printing collapses the cost of fidelity — a text copied by a press in Mainz in 1455 reaches Paris and Kraków with errors counted in dozens rather than hundreds. The internet collapses the cost of distance and revision — a draft written in Boston reaches Bangalore in seconds. Each technology adds to the prior layer rather than replacing it. Speech survives writing. Manuscript reading habits survive printing. Deep reading survives the internet, though all are reshaped.

The leitmotif this book has been tracking — cognitive tools as additive scaffoldings, not substitutes for the mind using them — is on display in language more vividly than anywhere else, because language was the first cognitive function we built scaffolding for, and we have been building ever since. The accumulated corpus of human writing — every book, every journal article, every letter, every legal record — is the substrate on which the large language models of Chapter 17 were trained. Those models have consumed more text than any human will read in a thousand lifetimes. What they have and have not thereby acquired is Chapter 17's question.

What language is, at the deepest level, is the capacity to make one's inner state available to another mind as a *shareable* thing — not merely as an impulse that drives behavior, but as a content that can be examined, labeled, combined with other contents, stored outside any single brain, and transmitted across time and space. The fourteen-month-old pointing at the basset hound is doing the earliest version of this: she is making her attention a shared object. Everything else that human language does — the grammar, the recursion, the displacement, the poetry, the mathematical notation, the legal system, the science — is an elaboration on that first gesture.

The chimpanzee can point at the banana. It cannot, as far as we know, point at the moon simply because the moon is there and someone else should see it.

That difference is the one this chapter has been about.

## Summary

**The key mechanism:** Human language is not a module or a brain region. It is a developmental program — a curriculum that begins with proto-conversations at two months, passes through joint attention at nine to twelve months, establishes declarative pointing by fourteen months, and produces question-asking by eighteen to twenty-four months. Each stage scaffolds the next. No other species runs this program.

**The key distinction:** Declarative reference — communicating for the purpose of sharing attention rather than directing behavior — is the cognitive signature that most sharply distinguishes human from non-human communication. Apes can point to request. They do not, in natural behavior or under extensive training, point to share.

**The key negative result:** Broca's area is not the language organ. *FOXP2* is not the language gene. The comparative anatomy and genetics are real; their popular interpretation is not. The neural signature of language is the expanded connectivity between frontal and temporal regions. The developmental substrate is the social curriculum, which is not localized anywhere.

**The key boundary case:** The honeybee waggle dance satisfies two of Hockett's key criteria (displacement, arbitrary mapping). It satisfies none of the rest. It is the most sophisticated non-human communication system we know of, and it is not language.

**The common mistake to watch for:** Treating symbol-referent learning as language. Apes can learn hundreds of symbols. That is impressive. It does not produce question-asking, recursive grammar, or declarative reference. The capacity to pair symbols with referents is not the capacity to build a grammar from those symbols and to ask whether the grammar is describing something true.

**What you should now be able to teach someone else:** Why declarative pointing matters, what the Nicaraguan Sign Language case proves about language as a biological drive, and why "Broca's area is the language organ" is a useful mnemonics for an introductory class and a misleading claim for anyone thinking carefully.

## Connections Forward

Chapter 16 examines collective intelligence — the capacity of groups to know and accomplish things that no individual could. It will argue that language is the enabling technology for the most powerful form of collective intelligence we know of: cumulative culture, the ability to build on the accumulated achievements of predecessors without reinventing them.

The contrast with Chapter 2's bacteria and Chapter 6's ants will be drawn precisely. Ant colonies achieve collective intelligence through stigmergy — behavioral marks left in the environment that coordinate other individuals' actions. Human cultures achieve collective intelligence through language — and specifically through the externalization of language in writing, which allows the accumulated knowledge of the dead to be available to the living. The difference between the two is not the sophistication of any individual; it is the fidelity and longevity of the transmission medium.

Chapter 17 will then return to artificial intelligence with a question that this chapter's framework makes precise: a system trained on the cumulative written output of human language is in contact with the record of collective intelligence. Whether it participates in the practice that generated that record — whether it can ask questions, share attention, and build on what it has read — is the question on which the chapter's evaluation will turn.

*What would change my mind: a demonstration that any non-human species, in its natural ecology and without laboratory training, spontaneously engages in declarative pointing or its functional equivalent — using a signal to direct another's attention to a referent for the sake of mutual interest, with no instrumental payoff. The closest documented cases (some chimpanzee gestures in mother-infant contexts) have not yet been shown to be reliably declarative rather than imperative. I would also revise on a credible demonstration of recursive syntax — genuinely unbounded, not just three-level embedding — in any non-human communicator.*

*Still puzzling: what produces the question. The eighteen-month-old who asks "Why?" is doing something that no amount of statistical learning straightforwardly predicts, because the question requires knowing not just that the caregiver has information she lacks, but that information-transmission is a legitimate purpose of communication — that minds can be used to fill other minds' gaps. I do not have a satisfying account of what cognitive ingredient distinguishes the child who asks from the ape who does not. Joint attention is necessary but not sufficient. Something more is required, and I am not confident the field has named it yet.*
