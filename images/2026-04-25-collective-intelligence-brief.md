## Concept

A composition that visualizes the chapter's central claim — *the bee swarm and the primate cortex implement structurally isomorphic decision-making algorithms on radically different substrates* — or, alternatively, the layered architecture (aggregation → coordination → cumulative culture) the chapter argues for.

## Composition options

**Option A — The swarm and the cortex.** A two-panel diagrammatic composition. Left panel: a honeybee swarm cluster on a tree branch, with arrows showing scouts departing in two competing directions, a vigorous waggle dance for site A in the foreground, a less vigorous dance for site B in the background, and small head-butt symbols indicating stop signals between rival dancers. A quorum-threshold annotation labels the takeoff transition. Right panel: a schematic of primate lateral intraparietal cortex (LIP) showing two evidence-accumulating populations of neurons (one tuned to leftward motion, one to rightward), with cross-inhibitory connections between them, and a threshold-triggered saccade output. The two panels share a single algorithmic schematic underneath: noisy evidence in → mutually-inhibitory accumulators → threshold → action. Caption: *the brain is in the relationships. In the bee colony, the relationships are between insects. In the cortex, between neurons. The algorithm is the same.*

**Option B — The three machines.** A field-guide composite layout with three panels showing the three mechanisms the chapter distinguishes. Panel one (Aggregation): a black-and-white photograph-style illustration of Galton's 1906 livestock fair at Plymouth, with crowds of guessers writing weights on tickets, and a small histogram inset showing the distribution of guesses centered on the true weight of the ox. Panel two (Coordination): a starling murmuration at dusk, with thin lines drawn between nearest-topological-neighbors to highlight the seven-neighbor coupling rule. Panel three (Cumulative culture): a montage of human stone tools across deep time — Oldowan flake (2.6 Mya) → Acheulean handaxe (1.7 Mya) → Levallois flake (300 kya) → Aurignacian blade (40 kya) → bronze knife → iron knife → modern surgical scalpel. Caption: *three architectures of collective intelligence, three different time scales. Aggregation is statistical. Coordination is informational. The ratchet is generational.*

**Option C — The record and the practice.** A two-panel composition. Left panel: a library wall of journals and books, neatly stacked, with a researcher reading at a desk. Right panel: a chemistry or biology bench in active use — a senior scientist standing beside a junior at the bench, the senior's hand on the junior's wrist as the junior pipettes a sample, a notebook open with crossed-out experiments. Caption: *the record is what travels. The practice is what makes it. A model trained on the left panel has read everything in the right panel's notebooks but has never stood at the bench.*

## Style

- Natural-history scientific illustration with diagrammatic elements. Honor the lineage of comparative-cognition figures from journal papers and the field-guide composite layout used in earlier chapters.
- Restrained palette. Black-and-white-and-one-color where possible.
- Avoid: glowing-brain icons, "AI" tropes, neural-net diagrams as decoration.
- For Option A, the cross-inhibitory schematic underneath should be drawn as a simple block diagram — not heavy graphic — so the algorithmic isomorphism is visible at a glance.

## Caption (Option A)

*Two implementations of the same decision-making algorithm. Left: a honeybee swarm choosing between competing nest sites uses cross-inhibitory stop signals between rival dance populations to break ties and converge on a single decision. Right: primate cortical decision-making in lateral intraparietal cortex uses mutually-inhibitory accumulator populations to reach a threshold and trigger an action. The architecture — noisy evidence in, mutually-inhibitory accumulators, threshold-triggered output — is the same. The substrate is not.*

## Caption (Option B)

*Three mechanisms wearing one phrase. Aggregation: independent estimates whose errors cancel (Galton's ox-weighing crowd, 1906). Coordination: information flowing through a flock at the speed of nearest-neighbor coupling (Ballerini 2008's topological starling rule). Cumulative culture: each generation starting from the modified output of the previous generation (Oldowan flake to surgical scalpel). Different species run different combinations. Only humans, on the evidence so far, run a cumulative ratchet at scale.*

## Caption (Option C)

*The record and the practice. The left side is what gets written down — the corpus of papers, books, and code that any reader can in principle access. The right side is what the writing comes from — the bench work, the apprenticeship, the failed experiments, the corrections that never made it to print. Polanyi's tacit knowledge lives almost entirely on the right. A system trained on the left has the corpus. It has not yet been shown to have the practice.*
