# Hero image brief — Epilogue: What the Nematode Knows

**Book:** intelligence (*Intelligence? A Natural History of Mind Across the Animal Kingdom*)
**Chapter slug:** epilogue
**Date:** 2026-04-25

## Concept

A composition that visualizes the book's structural close — *Caenorhabditis elegans*, complete in her niche, with her 302 neurons drawn at the level of detail the connectome literature actually provides. Or, alternatively, the map-not-ladder thesis as a single visual argument.

## Composition options

**Option A — The worm in detail.** A naturalistic-scientific illustration of an adult hermaphrodite *C. elegans* on agar, perhaps 1mm long, drawn from a side angle to show the body's translucency. Inside the body, the 302 neurons are sketched at low resolution — visible as small clusters in the head (amphid sensory neurons), nerve ring (the brain), ventral cord, and tail (lumbar ganglia). One small bacterial colony of *E. coli* is visible ahead of the worm in the direction she is moving. Caption: *Caenorhabditis elegans hermaphrodite, three days old, one millimeter long, 302 neurons. Each neuron in the figure is one cell — the connectome is at this resolution. The worm is moving up a faint chemical gradient toward the food, sampling, pivoting, integrating. She is doing exactly what her niche selects for. Nothing more. Nothing less.*

**Option B — The map.** A flat, restrained scientific-illustration map of cognitive niches, with each major species the book covered placed somewhere on a multi-axis space (rendered as a 2D projection of the 14 axes). The bacterium is in one corner. C. elegans is near it but slightly removed. Pick a few exemplars — slime mold, planarian, Aplysia, octopus, scrub jay, cleaner wrasse, dog, dolphin, chimpanzee, human — each placed by their cognitive profile. AI is shown as a separate marker labeled *(non-biological)* in a different color, in a region of the map that no biological organism occupies. Caption: *the phylogenetic map of cognition. Each species occupies a region of niche-fitted capacities. There is no single ordering. There is no top. There is only a map, drawn to whatever resolution our methods allow.*

**Option C — The closing image.** A simple, almost minimalist composition. A worm, drawn to scale, with one small period after her — a literal full stop. Above and around her, scattered very lightly across the page, the names of all the other species the book has covered, each in its own small typographic patch — Cataglyphis ant, scrub jay, dolphin, chimpanzee, cleaner wrasse, honeybee, slime mold, planarian, octopus, magpie, gorilla, bonobo, Asian elephant. The human is one of these scattered names, no more emphasized than the others. The worm is in the center, alone, fully drawn, the rest implied. Caption: *one species. One niche. One complete solution. The whole book in a single illustration.*

## Style

- Natural-history scientific illustration, perhaps slightly more delicate and reflective than the chapter heroes.
- Restrained palette. Black-and-white-and-one-color where possible.
- For Option A, the worm should be drawn at the level of care comparative-anatomy figures use — beautiful, accurate, technical, not cute.
- Avoid: glowing-brain icons, "AI" tropes, neural-net diagrams as decoration, anything that could be read as anthropomorphizing the worm.

## Caption (Option A)

*Caenorhabditis elegans hermaphrodite, three days old, one millimeter long, 302 neurons. The connectome was first published by White, Southgate, Thomson and Brenner in 1986; it remains, almost forty years later, one of two complete animal connectomes in the literature. The worm is moving up a chemical gradient toward food. She is doing exactly what her niche selects for. Nothing more. Nothing less.*

## Caption (Option B)

*The phylogenetic map of cognition. Each species occupies a region of capacities fitted to the problems it faces. There is no single ordering. There is no top. The artificial systems we are now building occupy a region of the map that no biological lineage has previously occupied — a different niche, with different selection pressures, and a different shape.*

## Caption (Option C)

*One species, one niche, one complete solution. The book ends here, with the same organism it began with — Caenorhabditis elegans, doing what nervous systems do when they are well-fitted to their problems. Three hundred and two neurons are enough for her. The question of how many are enough for what is the question this book has been about.*
