# Hero image brief — Chapter 6: Pattern Recognition and Perception

**Book:** intelligence (*Intelligence? A Natural History of Mind Across the Animal Kingdom*)
**Chapter slug:** pattern-recognition
**Date:** 2026-04-25

## Concept

A composition that visualizes the chapter's central architectural argument — *the cortex as auto-associative engine* — by showing input fibers expanding into a sparse cortical sheet with recurrent collaterals doing pattern completion. Or, alternatively, the chapter's hook scene: an archerfish picking the trained face from a lineup.

## Composition options

**Option A — The face lineup.** A naturalistic illustration of an archerfish hovering in the upper portion of a tank, looking up. Above the water surface, ten or more human-face photographs are arrayed in a grid. A small jet of water arcs from the fish's mouth toward the trained face, which is faintly highlighted. Below, a small inset shows the fish's brain in cross-section with the lateral pallium colored in to mark where the recognition happens. Caption: *the fish has no fusiform face area; the lateral pallium is doing the work*.

**Option B — The cortical circuit.** A schematic of the three-operation auto-associative architecture. Left: input fibers (a few hundred, color-coded by receptor type). Middle: dimensionality-expanded cortical sheet (tens of thousands of pyramidal neurons), with lateral inhibitory interneurons drawn explicitly. Right: recurrent collateral connections among the pyramidal neurons, with strengthened ones drawn thicker. Three labeled call-outs: *expansion*, *separation*, *completion*. Visual argument: the same circuit does both halves of the discrimination/generalization problem.

**Option C — The rotated frog.** A small grid of nine images: a plastic-toy frog photographed at 0°, 90°, 180°, 270° in three rotation planes. Below, a goldfish poised at the choice point. The fish's body language indicates the choice has been made. Caption: *the goldfish recognizes the same object across viewpoints it has never seen — invariance is a property of the cortex, not of the image*.

## Style

- Natural-history scientific illustration. Honor the lineage of Cajal's Golgi-stained cortical drawings (for Option B) and the WormAtlas/journal-figure aesthetic for Options A and C.
- Restrained palette. Black-and-white-and-one-color (orange for the fish, sepia for the brain).
- Avoid: glowing-brain icons, neural-net diagrams as decoration, "AI" tropes.

## Caption (Option A)

*An archerfish discriminates a familiar face from forty-three new ones with eighty-percent accuracy. It has no fusiform face area, no neocortex, and no evolutionary reason to care about human faces. The vertebrate pallium recognizes faces the way it recognizes everything else.*

## Caption (Option B)

*The cortical recognition circuit. A few hundred input fibers project diffusely onto tens of thousands of pyramidal neurons. Lateral inhibition keeps the active subset sparse, separating overlapping inputs. Recurrent collaterals, shaped by Hebbian plasticity, complete partial inputs by reference to stored patterns. Discrimination and generalization in one architecture.*

## Caption (Option C)

*Goldfish viewpoint invariance. Trained at 0° on a turtle and a frog, the fish recognizes both at every rotation in every plane it has been tested in. The image changes; the recognition does not.*
