## Concept

A composition that visualizes the chapter's central empirical result — *a great ape anticipating, with her eyes, that an actor will search where the actor falsely believes the object to be* — by rendering the eye-tracker output overlaid on the video frame the ape was watching.

## Composition options

**Option A — The eye-tracker frame.** A still frame from the Krupenye 2016 video, showing the actor in the act of returning to search for an object. Two locations are visible in the frame — the location where the object actually is, and the location where the actor falsely believes it to be. A small heatmap overlay shows where the ape's gaze was pooling in anticipation: at the *false-belief* location. A small inset shows the eye-tracker hardware and the ape's face during the experiment. Caption: *the ape's gaze anticipated the actor's behavior — at the place the actor falsely believed the object to be*.

**Option B — The four layers.** A schematic four-tier diagram showing the layered structure of theory of mind: goal attribution at the bottom (an arrow connecting an agent to a target), perspective-taking above (an agent with a "field of view" indicated), implicit false belief above that (the actor and the object in different actual-vs-believed positions), explicit recursive mentalizing at the top (a thought-bubble within a thought-bubble). Each layer labeled with which species are known to demonstrate it. Caption: *theory of mind is not one capacity but a hierarchy of related ones, with different species clearing different boundaries*.

**Option C — The IRL parallel.** A two-panel comparison. Left: a person reaching for a glass of water; arrows show the inference path from observed behavior to inferred reward (thirst) to inferred belief (water in glass). Right: an inverse-reinforcement-learning algorithm with the same observation-and-inference structure, computing latent reward and world-model parameters from observed action sequences. Caption: *theory of mind is biological inverse reinforcement learning — inferring the reward function and world-model that explain another agent's behavior*.

## Caption (Option A)

*A bonobo watches a video. An actor is returning to search for an object that has been moved while the actor was out of the room. The bonobo's gaze, recorded by an infrared eye-tracker, is pooling at the location where the actor falsely believes the object to be — not at the location where the bonobo herself knows the object actually is. She is modeling the actor's belief about the world.*

## Caption (Option B)

*The hierarchy of theory of mind. Goal attribution is widely shared. Perspective-taking is harder. Implicit false-belief reasoning is rarer still. Explicit recursive mentalizing — what a seven-year-old child does casually — has not yet been clearly demonstrated outside humans.*

## Caption (Option C)

*Theory of mind as inverse reinforcement learning. Observation: the agent reaches for the glass. Inference: thirst (the reward function), water in the glass (the world-model). The compression is enormous — without it, social learning would require memorizing every action sequence; with it, learning that this person is thirsty explains hundreds of distinct behaviors.*
