## Concept

A composition that visualizes the chapter's central experimental logic — the *cognitive-bias test as a window into invertebrate affect* — by showing a bee at the moment of decision in front of an ambiguous stimulus, with the two trained alternatives flanking it.

## Composition options

**Option A — The Bateson assay.** A clean three-cylinder layout viewed from above. Left cylinder: trained-positive odor (sucrose), color-coded with one shade. Right cylinder: trained-negative odor (quinine), color-coded with another. Center cylinder: ambiguous test odor, somewhere on the continuum between. A single bumblebee in mid-flight, hovering near the center cylinder, captured at the decision moment. Two side-callouts: one labeled "shaken" with an arrow showing the bee biased toward the negative interpretation; one labeled "sucrose-fed" with an arrow showing bias toward the positive. Visual argument: the same ambiguous cue, two different priors, two different decisions. Caption: *the bee's interpretation tilts toward whichever way her recent experience has tipped her*.

**Option B — The fluphenazine-block.** A two-panel composition. Panel one: bumblebee, post-sucrose, approaching the ambiguous cylinder. Panel two: bumblebee, post-sucrose-plus-fluphenazine, hesitating. Below each panel, the same molecular cascade — sucrose → mushroom-body Kenyon cells → dopamine release — with the dopamine receptor either active (panel 1) or blocked (panel 2). The visual argument is that the affective state is dopamine-dependent: cut the dopamine and the state goes with it.

**Option C — The cryptic predator.** A naturalistic illustration of a bumblebee hovering above a flower, with a faint outline of a crab spider that is camouflaged in the flower's color. The bee's posture suggests inspection rather than approach. Below: a small graph showing inspection time on dangerous-looking flowers compared to safe-looking ones. The visual argument is the false-alarm signature — the bee is paying real foraging cost for an interpretive bias.

## Style

- Natural-history scientific illustration. Honor Cajal-style anatomical clarity and the lineage of behavioral-ecology figures from *Behavioral Ecology and Sociobiology*.
- Restrained palette. Yellow/orange for bee, two complementary colors for the trained cylinders, neutral background.
- Avoid: emoji-style affective icons (the *sad face* / *happy face* iconography), neural-net diagrams, "AI" tropes.

## Caption (Option A)

*The cognitive-bias test. A bumblebee trained on two odors — one positive, one negative — must decide what to make of an ambiguous third. Her interpretation depends on her recent experience: a shake tips her toward threat, a sucrose drop tips her toward reward. The bias is the chapter's window into invertebrate affect.*

## Caption (Option B)

*Optimism as a state, not a movement. The bee's optimistic bias depends on dopamine. Block the receptor with fluphenazine and the bias vanishes — even though the sucrose, the training, and the cylinders are all the same. The state is real because cutting its molecular substrate cuts the behavior.*

## Caption (Option C)

*The pessimistic bee pays a real cost. Bumblebees that have learned a flower patch can hide cryptic predators show false alarms — hesitating at safe flowers, foraging less efficiently, treating ambiguous information as dangerous. The interpretation has tilted, and the energy budget has tilted with it.*
