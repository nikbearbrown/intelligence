# Hero image brief — Chapter 4: Learning and Memory

**Book:** intelligence (*Intelligence? A Natural History of Mind Across the Animal Kingdom*)
**Chapter slug:** learning-and-memory

## Concept

A composition that visualizes the chapter's central mechanism — *the synapse that remembers* — by showing the *Aplysia* sensory-to-motor synapse in two states: before sensitization and after long-term sensitization. The reader should see, in one image, that the synapse has *physically changed* as a result of experience.

## Composition options

**Option A — The synapse, before and after.** A two-panel scientific illustration. Panel one (left) shows a sensory neuron's terminal contacting a motor neuron at a single bouton with a small number of vesicle-release sites. Panel two (right) shows the same synapse after long-term sensitization: more vesicle-release sites, a larger active zone, and additional synaptic varicosities branching off. A small color-coded inset traces the molecular cascade (5-HT → cAMP → PKA → K⁺ channel modulation → Ca²⁺ → glutamate) with the nuclear translocation step (PKA → CREB-1 → gene transcription) labeled as the gateway to the structural change. Caption: *the same synapse, after experience*.

**Option B — *Aplysia* with the gill-withdrawal reflex visualized.** A naturalist-illustration of *Aplysia californica* in profile, with the gill in two states: extended (relaxed) and retracted (post-shock). Annotation arrows trace the reflex circuit: siphon → sensory neuron → motor neuron → gill muscle, with the serotonergic interneuron labeled where it modulates the sensory terminal. Honors the natural-history register and grounds the molecular abstraction in a recognizable animal.

**Option C — The CREB brake.** A schematic of a single nucleus showing CREB-1 (activator, in one color) and CREB-2 (repressor, in another color) competing for the same DNA binding sites. PKA arriving at the nucleus phosphorylates CREB-1 and (via additional pathways) helps remove CREB-2. The visual argument is that long-term memory is not stored by accumulation but by *gated transcription*. Caption: *the brake on long-term memory*.

## Style

- Natural-history scientific illustration, restrained palette.
- Honor the lineage of Cajal's neuronal drawings and the original Kandel-lab anatomical diagrams.
- Avoid: glowing-brain icons, stylized neural-net diagrams, "AI" tropes.
- Where the molecular cascade is shown, use clear arrow notation; do not let the diagram become a wiring chart.

## Caption (Option A)

*Long-term memory in Aplysia is not stored in a stronger signal but in a structurally larger synapse. The molecular cascade that converts a tail-shock into a permanent change in connection runs from a serotonin pulse, through cyclic AMP and protein kinase A, to the nucleus, where the transcription factor CREB activates the genes that build new connection sites.*

## Caption (Option B)

*Aplysia californica, with the gill-withdrawal reflex circuit traced. The sensory neuron of the siphon contacts the motor neuron of the gill at a single, identifiable synapse. Modulation of that synapse by serotonin from a third neuron, after a tail-shock, is the molecular substrate of memory.*

## Caption (Option C)

*Long-term memory is gated. CREB-1 activates the genes whose products grow new synapses; CREB-2 represses those same genes. The brain has a brake on long-term memory because committing every passing event to permanent structure would saturate the synaptic machinery within hours.*
