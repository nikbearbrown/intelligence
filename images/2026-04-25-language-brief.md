## Concept

A composition that visualizes the chapter's central asymmetry — *human children spontaneously perform declarative pointing while apes, in their natural ecology and under intensive language training, do not* — or, alternatively, the developmental curriculum (proto-conversation → joint attention → declarative pointing → question) that produces the human language phenotype.

## Composition options

**Option A — The pointing finger.** A two-panel naturalistic illustration. Left panel: a fourteen-month-old human infant in a park, pointing at a dog. Her face is turned not toward the dog but toward her mother — checking that her mother is looking at the dog. The triadic geometry (child–object–adult, with the eye contact line between child and adult clearly drawn) is the composition's load-bearing detail. Right panel: a captive chimpanzee infant in a research enclosure, with a plate of fruit visible and a researcher beside it. The chimp is reaching toward the fruit (imperative gesture) but her gaze is on the fruit, not on the researcher. Caption: *the same age, the same triadic situation, two different gestures. The human points and looks at the adult; the chimp reaches for the food.*

**Option B — The four-stage curriculum.** A field-guide composite layout with four panels showing the developmental sequence in human infants. Panel one (2–4 months): an infant and a parent face-to-face, mouths open mid-vocalization, eye contact, the turn-taking caption "proto-conversation." Panel two (9–12 months): infant and parent both looking at a third object — a colored toy on a play mat — with the gaze lines visibly converging on the toy. Caption: "joint attention." Panel three (12 months): infant pointing at a window or sky, looking back at the parent for confirmation. Caption: "declarative pointing." Panel four (18–24 months): a slightly older child pointing at something off-frame and producing a question word ("what's that?") visibly above her head. Caption: "the question." Each panel labeled with age, and the chimp counterpart for each stage shown in a smaller inset, faded, with "absent" or "minimal." Caption: *the human developmental program, four stages. No other species has been documented to run this sequence.*

**Option C — Nicaraguan Sign Language.** A photograph-style composition showing a small group of deaf Nicaraguan schoolchildren, perhaps in the schoolyard at the Centro Nacional Cristiano para Sordos in Managua, signing to each other. The composition foregrounds two children mid-conversation, with a third child watching and learning. The viewing angle includes other children also signing in the background, suggesting the cohort. Caption: *between 1979 and 1986, a population of deaf Nicaraguan children pooled their idiosyncratic home signs into a fully grammatical signed language. Within one generation, the youngest cohort produced spatial agreement, recursive structures, and the structural complexity of any natural human language.*

## Style

- Natural-history scientific illustration. Honor the lineage of developmental-psychology figures.
- Restrained palette. Black-and-white-and-one-color where possible.
- Avoid: glowing-brain icons, "AI" tropes, neural-net diagrams as decoration.
- For Option A specifically, the gaze lines should be drawn — not heavy graphic arrows, but clear enough that the triadic geometry is visible at a glance.

## Caption (Option A)

*The same age, the same triadic situation, two different gestures. The fourteen-month-old human points at a thing and checks her mother's face — sharing the fact of the dog. The young chimpanzee reaches for the fruit she wants. After three decades of intensive comparative research, the asymmetry has held: humans point declaratively without prompting, no other species has been documented to.*

## Caption (Option B)

*The human developmental program for language, four stages. Each stage is a behavioral signature established by a specific age range and missing in great-ape rearing. The program is not a single capacity. It is a sequence in which each stage scaffolds the next.*

## Caption (Option C)

*The Nicaraguan school-children whose home signs grammaticized into Idioma de Señas de Nicaragua over the course of a single generation. The youngest cohort drove the grammaticization. Senghas and Coppola documented spatial agreement, verb morphology, and recursive structure within ten years of the school's opening. The drive to grammaticize symbolic communication is, on the evidence, a biological imperative on the order of walking.*
