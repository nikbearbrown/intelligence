## Concept

The book's structural close — a return to *Caenorhabditis elegans*, the 302-neuron nematode that opened the empirical walk in Chapter 3 (Steering). Walks the worm's actual cognitive capacities against the book's fourteen-axis catalog and shows that the worm's profile is *complete in its niche* — not a smaller version of human cognition but a different solution fitted to a different problem space. Closes on the structural argument the book has been building: the phylogenetic ladder is a map of niche-fittings, not a hierarchy with humans at the top. Intelligence is plural. The question mark stays.

## Specification move

The Epilogue does not introduce new specifications. It compresses and applies the structural argument the book has built across eighteen chapters to a single organism. The conceptual move is from *catalog of capacities* (the fourteen axes the book has built) to *map of niche-solutions* (each capacity present in some species because that species' niche selected for it, absent in others because their niches did not). The worm is the cleanest case for the map metaphor: a complete organism with a sharp profile (high on basic axes, zero on high-level axes), exquisitely fitted to a niche where the high-level axes are not selected for.

## Mechanism for the deep-dive

There is no "deep-dive" in the chapter-mechanical sense — the Epilogue compresses material the book has already built. The structural argument is: the worm's 302-neuron profile is *complete* in its niche, not partial. This is the same argument made for every species in the book, applied here as the closing example. The catalog walked: steering yes, learning yes, prediction yes, perception yes, navigation in the small yes, simulation no, theory of mind no, creativity no, mirror-test no, metacognition no, language no, cumulative culture no — and each absence is *not a deficit* but the absence of selection pressure for that capacity in this niche.

## Central analogy

*The phylogenetic ladder as a map, not a hierarchy.* The analogy is the book's load-bearing metaphor and the chapter is built around naming it explicitly. It does productive work — readers raised on the *scala naturae* expect a single ordering and the chapter argues that no such ordering exists. Where it breaks: maps still have features that can be ordered (north-south, east-west); the cognitive map is multi-dimensional in a way that resists 2D visualization. The breakdown is named in §3 ("the cognitive map is multi-dimensional in a way that resists single-axis ordering").

## Risks flagged

- **Tone risk.** The Epilogue could become sentimental. The chapter is calibrated toward the natural-history register the book has used throughout — observational, reflective, but not effusive. The worm is a worm; she is not symbolic of anything beyond herself.
- **Risk of over-claiming the worm's "completeness."** The phrase "complete in her niche" could be read as romantic. The chapter is honest that the worm's *biological* completeness in her niche does not mean her cognition is *unimprovable* in all environments — it means it is well-fitted to *this* environment, which is the structural point.
- **Risk of dismissing AI's place in the framework.** The Epilogue does not return to AI. Ch. 18 closed that thread. The Epilogue is for the biological argument that the book has been building. AI's placement was Ch. 17's job.
- **The map-not-ladder framing.** Stephen Jay Gould and others have argued for this view for decades; the chapter does not pretend the framing is original. It says the framing is what the seventeen-chapter walk supports, regardless of who said it first.
