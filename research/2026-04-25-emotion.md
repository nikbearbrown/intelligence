## Concept

Emotion in invertebrates, treated through two operational frameworks: the Anderson-Adolphs (2014) emotion primitives and the Mendl-Burman-Paul (2010) cognitive-bias-as-Bayesian-prior framework. The chapter argues that under the *functional* definition (Definition X) — a persistent valenced internal state that biases interpretation of ambiguous stimuli — bees, some crustaceans, and certain cockroach preparations have emotion; under the *subjective* definition (Definition Y), the question cannot be settled with current methods. Both answers are informative.

## Specification move

Three jobs of the word *emotion* unbundled before any empirical work:

1. **Emotion as feeling** — the subjective experience. Unanswerable from outside.
2. **Emotion as behavior** — the outward signs (flinch, freeze, attack, withdraw). Measurable but not by itself sufficient.
3. **Emotion as functional state** — an internal nervous-system condition meeting specific operational criteria. The chapter's working definition.

The Anderson-Adolphs primitives (persistence, scalability, valence, generalization, coordination) operationalize sense (3) without requiring sense (1). Mendl-Paul cognitive bias gives us the test from outside.

## The mechanism for the deep-dive

*Perry, Baciadonna, Chittka 2016 — sucrose-induced optimistic bias in bumblebees, blocked by fluphenazine.* The mechanism is the cleanest piece of invertebrate-emotion evidence in the literature: a behavioral signature (faster approach to ambiguous cylinder) that satisfies all five Anderson-Adolphs primitives, paired with a pharmacological intervention that ties the state to a specific neuromodulator (dopamine). One sentence: *the bumblebee's optimism is a persistent dopamine-dependent state, identifiable by a Bayesian-prior shift in interpretation, and falsifiable by blocking the receptor that mediates it.*

## Central analogy

*Affective state as Bayesian prior.* This is the load-bearing analogy of the chapter, drawn from Mendl-Paul-Burman 2010. A negative mood shifts the prior toward danger; a positive mood shifts it toward reward. Where it breaks down: an actual Bayesian prior is updated continuously by every relevant observation, while affective states have characteristic time-constants (minutes to hours for mood, seconds for emotion proper) that make them stickier than ideal Bayesian inference would justify. The break-down is small enough that the analogy survives and pays the chapter's central claim.

## Risks flagged

- **The dopamine-vs-octopamine controversy is presented in compressed form.** I gave Chittka's reading more weight than Giurfa's because the fluphenazine result is, by my reading, harder to explain on the pure-motivation account. If review wants the Giurfa side strengthened, §5.3 is where it goes.
- **The headless cockroach result is genuinely strange.** I treat it as the chapter's open puzzle in "Still puzzling." The literature on what the thoracic ganglion is computing is thinner than the chapter's prose may imply.
- **Forel's ant observations are referenced briefly without URL.** They are historical (late 19th / early 20th century) and well-attested in the comparative literature but do not have a clean primary citation in modern peer-reviewed form. Mentioned as context, not as load-bearing.
- **The Cambridge Declaration 2012 and New York Declaration 2024 are statements of scientific consensus, not single-experiment findings.** I cite them as positions, not as evidence — appropriate given their function in the chapter's ethical framing.
