## Concept

What language actually requires beyond communication: the structural cluster (productivity, displacement, recursion, duality of patterning), the cognitive shift from imperative to declarative reference, and the developmental social curriculum (proto-conversations → joint attention → declarative pointing → questions) that scaffolds child language acquisition and is not present in any non-human species. The chapter argues that language is not a brain area, not a single gene, and not the presence of vocalization — it is the simultaneous co-occurrence of a feature cluster supported by a developmental program no other species runs.

## Specification move

Three things unbundled before empirical work:

1. **Communication vs. language.** Communication is information transfer that affects another's behavior — bees, vervets, plants all do it. Language is a particular cluster of structural features. Hockett's 13 design features map the boundary; HCF 2002 narrowed the claim to recursion. Most non-human cases capture some features without the cluster.
2. **Imperative vs. declarative reference.** Imperative = "give me that"; declarative = "look at that, isn't that interesting." Apes do imperative reliably. Apes do not, in the natural ethogram or under language training, produce spontaneous declarative pointing. The transition is the cognitive threshold and connects directly to joint attention (Ch. 10/11).
3. **Emotional vocalization vs. language system.** Two distinct neural systems established by clean clinical double dissociation — Broca's aphasia preserves laughing/crying; pseudobulbar affect destroys emotional control while preserving language. Chimpanzee calls are subcortical; human language is neocortical. Looking at chimp calls for the seeds of human syntax is comparing apples to oranges.

## Mechanism for the deep-dive

*The developmental social curriculum, with the dual-system neural picture as a brushstroke, and the ape ceiling as a counterfactual.* §15.3 walks through proto-conversations (2-4 mo) → joint attention (9-12 mo) → declarative pointing (12 mo) → questions (18-24 mo), then turns to Saffran's statistical learning result as the under-the-hood power of the human child's brain. Then the ape ceiling: Project Nim, Kanzi, the consistent absence of recursive grammar, displacement, and questions. Then the genetic/neural sketch: Cantalupo-Hopkins on Broca's area, Lai et al. on FOXP2 KE family, with the careful reading that none of these is "the language organ." The chapter's load-bearing claim: language is a developmental program whose ingredients include the cortex, the gene, and the curriculum together. Take any one ingredient away and the program does not run.

## Central analogy

*Language as a developmental program rather than an organ or a feature.* The analogy does productive work — readers expect language to live somewhere (Broca's area, the LAD, the FOXP2 gene), and the chapter argues against each candidate localization in favor of the program metaphor. Where it breaks: programs in the computer-science sense are deterministic and reproducible, while developmental programs are probabilistic and modulated by environment. The breakdown is named in the closing of §15.3 ("take any one of the ingredients away and the program does not run"), which gestures toward the right kind of program — a multi-component biological developmental sequence — without overstating the analogy.

## Risks flagged

- **HCF 2002 contested.** Hauser-Chomsky-Fitch's recursion-as-FLN claim was challenged by Pinker & Jackendoff 2005 and subsequent work. The chapter names the contestation explicitly rather than presenting HCF as settled.
- **Cantalupo-Hopkins 2001 partially contested.** Subsequent MRI studies have not always replicated the gray-matter asymmetry; the chapter acknowledges this implicitly by calling the Broca homolog "anatomically and cytoarchitecturally homologous" without claiming functional equivalence.
- **FOXP2 popular-press overclaim.** "Language gene" framing is wrong. The chapter is careful to call FOXP2 "one of many genes whose normal function is required for the human language phenotype to develop."
- **The "Kansi vs Kanzi" item from the brief was DROPPED.** The Zulu cooking-pot etymology claim could not be verified and looked like a fabrication or confusion. Better to omit than risk it.
- **Plant communication via mycorrhizal networks** mentioned in passing — Suzanne Simard work is real but the strongest claims are contested. The chapter uses the example as a "communication is widely distributed" data point, not as a load-bearing claim.
- **Honeybee waggle dance interpretation.** Riley 2005 settled the basic encoding question. Whether the dance is "really" symbolic remains a matter of taxonomy (does displacement-without-productivity count?). The chapter says it doesn't count as language but is real partial-symbolic communication.
