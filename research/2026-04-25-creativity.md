## Concept

Creativity as the capacity in this book most resistant to operational definition and most vulnerable to anthropomorphic projection. The chapter argues that real evidence exists for tool innovation in chimps, corvids, and octopuses, and for sustained aesthetic decision-making in bowerbirds and a handful of trained chimpanzees, but that single-individual single-trial anecdotes have repeatedly turned out to be over-interpretations once careful follow-up was done. *Cumulative* creativity (innovations building on previous innovations across generations) may be the thing that is genuinely uniquely human.

## Specification move

Three things unbundled before empirical work:

1. **Novelty + utility + intentionality** — the three components most operational definitions converge on. Each is harder to measure than it looks.
2. **Psychological vs historical creativity** — new to the individual vs new to the species or culture. Comparative work mostly measures the first.
3. **Product vs process** — most experiments measure artifacts (the bent wire, the spear); most theories are about cognitive operations (attention, generative goals). Honest interpretation has to keep both in view.

## The mechanism for the deep-dive

*Tool manufacture across four lineages — corvids, primates, cephalopods, bowerbirds — as the empirically anchored core of non-human creativity.* §12.3 walks each case carefully: Hunt 1996 wild NC crow hooks → von Bayern 2018 captive compound tools → Pruetz 2007 Fongoli chimp spears → Finn 2009 octopus coconut-shells → Endler-Kelley bowerbird forced-perspective. Each shares the operational structure: attention to a future state, manipulation to a held template, evaluation against the goal. One sentence: *the artifacts in each case are produced by an animal that, at some level, was thinking about the result.*

## Central analogy

*Innovation as variant-and-template.* The held template is a goal state; the variant is the candidate produced through manipulation; the comparison-to-template is the evaluation. Where the analogy breaks: for human cumulative culture, the template itself is shaped by previous innovations, producing the open-ended ratchet that the chapter argues may be uniquely human; for non-human creativity, the templates appear to be largely fixed by the lineage's evolution and individual history. The break is exactly the chapter's "what would change my mind" condition.

## Risks flagged

- **Forbidden phrase "obviously" caught and replaced with "clearly".**
- **Two URL parens issues** — Cell URLs for Pruetz 2007 and Finn 2009 had `(07)` and `(09)` that broke markdown rendering. Both swapped to PubMed equivalents.
- **Bowerbird citation** — used Kelley & Endler 2012 *PNAS* as the primary citation. The 2010 Endler-Endler-Doerr *Current Biology* paper was the original; the 2012 paper extended the result with individual-quality variation. If review wants the 2010 cited instead, it is at PubMed 20832314.
- **Betty wire-bending result** treated with caveats. Original Weir-Chappell-Kacelnik 2002 *Science* paper was sensational; later work (Bluff, Troscianko, Weir, Kacelnik 2010 and others) showed wild NC crows engage in similar object-shaping, complicating the *spontaneous insight* framing. The chapter follows the post-2010 reading.
- **Congo and the chimpanzee paintings** — referenced compactly. The interpretation as art is largely Desmond Morris's (1962 *The Biology of Art*), and later work has not strongly contradicted or confirmed his framing. The chapter follows the Chapter 1 commitment to report behavior carefully and let the reader decide what to call it.
- **The cultural-ratchet uniqueness claim** is the chapter's most contested synthetic argument and is named directly in "What would change my mind." The chimpanzee tool-use traditions and corvid hook-tool diversity show *some* cumulative effects, but not at the human level.
