## Concept

The first nervous system that operationally meets the criteria for goal-directed behavior. *C. elegans* extends the bacterial chemotaxis trick from Ch. 2 with three additions: (a) a bilateral body plan that simplifies navigation to forward-or-turn, (b) labeled-line sensory neurons with default valences, (c) a neuromodulator layer (dopamine, serotonin) that produces persistent internal states. The chapter argues that these three additions, together with the bacterial dC/dt logic, mutual-inhibition circuits, and associative plasticity, constitute the *minimum architecture* for goal-directed behavior.

## Specification move

The chapter pulls apart three things that get conflated in popular treatments of "what does a worm do":

1. **Sensing vs. integration.** Detecting a chemical is not deciding what to do about it. The worm's labeled lines (ASE, AWC, ASH, AFD, etc.) sense; the interneurons (AIY, AIA, AIB, RIM, etc.) integrate. The HEN-1 mutant phenotype (Ishihara 2002) directly demonstrates the separability.
2. **Stimulus vs. internal state.** The worm at the copper barrier behaves differently when starved than when fed. Same external information, different decision. This is the chapter's load-bearing distinction between reactive and goal-directed behavior.
3. **Reflex vs. labeled response.** A reflex is a fixed sensory-motor coupling. A labeled-line response carries default valence into the integration center but is *modifiable* by neuromodulators and by experience. The worm has labeled lines, not reflexes.

## The mechanism for the deep-dive

The deep-dive is *how the worm steers*. The mechanism builds across §3.3 in three layers: (i) the bacterial dC/dt rule extended to multiple labeled-line sensory channels feeding shared interneurons; (ii) the neuromodulator layer (dopamine for local food signal, serotonin for satiety/state), which sets the gain on each labeled line on a slower timescale than the synaptic activity; (iii) the integration node — exemplified by HEN-1 — that combines conflicting cues into a coherent decision. One sentence: *the worm steers by computing the bacterial chemotaxis rule on multiple sensory channels at once, weighted by neuromodulator-encoded internal state, with a distinct integration mechanism that is itself dissectable from the sensory machinery.*

## Central analogy

*The Roomba is the worm in plastic.* Used in the Extension Note and called back in the chapter's closing reasoning. Where the analogy breaks: the Roomba does not learn that one corner of the kitchen is always sticky; it has no neuromodulator to widen its options when its bin is full; it has no equivalent of starvation that would make it accept higher risk for higher reward. The break-down is exactly the chapter's point — the worm has six architectural components, the Roomba has a subset, and the missing components are what give *Caenorhabditis* the moods that the Roomba lacks.

## Risks flagged

- **The "six-component minimum architecture" claim is the chapter's most contestable.** It is a synthesis I am defending, not a result from a single paper. The "What would change my mind" line names the falsification criterion. Reviewers may want to challenge the cleanness of the list.
- **The well-fed-vs-starved copper trade-off is presented as a clean experimental result.** It is well-established as a phenomenon, but the precise mechanism by which hunger tips the trade-off involves multiple papers and is not as cleanly shown in a single experiment as the chapter's prose implies. If review wants tighter sourcing, I'd cite a specific food-state-modulates-trade-off paper here.
- **Brenner's 1974 paper does not itself claim that 302 neurons is the right number** — that came from White 1986. I cite both, but the chapter's "302" framing is post-hoc to Brenner's choice of organism.
- **The Witvliet 2021 caveat about synaptic variability** is included in §3.4 because it would be misleading to leave the connectome story at the 1986 endpoint.
