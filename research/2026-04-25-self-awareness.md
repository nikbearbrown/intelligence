## Concept

The mirror test (Gallup 1970) and what it actually measures — visual self-recognition supported by an internal body model and contingency detection. The chapter argues that *self-awareness* is at least three layered capacities (body self, social self, narrative self), that the mirror test cleanly measures only the first, and that the function (visual self-recognition) is conserved across phylogenetically distant lineages on radically different brain anatomies.

## Specification move

Three things unbundled before empirical work:

1. **Body self vs social self vs narrative self.** Three distinct self-models. The mirror test measures the first; the second was largely covered in Ch. 10; the third is largely human and requires autobiographical memory.
2. **Visual-kinesthetic match vs deeper consciousness claims.** What the mirror test actually requires is a comparison between proprioceptive self-motion and visual feed showing same-motion. That is a real cognitive capacity. The metaphysical *consciousness* claims often made on the basis of mirror-test passage are not directly supported by the test alone.
3. **Failure asymmetry.** Passing the test is strong evidence for the body self. Failing is weak evidence against it, because failure can come from sensory mismatch, social inhibition, or motivation rather than absence of self-model.

## Mechanism for the deep-dive

*The four-stage Gallup protocol — social response → physical inspection → contingency testing → self-directed behavior — paired with the phylogenetic catalog of passers.* §13.3 walks through chimpanzees, gorillas (failure case), dolphins, elephants, magpies, cleaner wrasses. Each species' result is interpreted against what the test actually requires (visual-kinesthetic match, body model, contingency detection). The chapter's load-bearing claim: function conserved across radically different anatomies. One sentence: *the cleaner wrasse and the chimpanzee are doing the same operational job — comparing motor commands to visual feed, recognizing the match, and using the recognition to direct behavior — on neural hardware separated by 400+ million years of evolution.*

## Central analogy

*Self-awareness as a layered phenomenon, with the mirror test measuring only one layer.* Used throughout. The body self (motor planning), social self (Ch. 10), and narrative self (autobiographical memory) come apart cleanly in clinical cases. The mirror test is the cleanest behavioral assay for layer one. Where the analogy breaks: the layers are not strictly hierarchical — some animals appear to have rich social selves without strong narrative selves, and clinical cases like alien hand syndrome show that the body self itself can fragment into ownership and agency components. The breakdown is exactly what the clinical syndromes section is for.
