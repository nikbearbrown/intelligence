## Concept

Social intelligence as a layered phenomenon with three distinct cognitive capacities (emotional contagion, empathy, theory of mind) and a convergent evolutionary distribution across primates, cetaceans, and elephants. The chapter argues that the function (managing complex social environments) is conserved across phylogenetically distant lineages while the substrate (anatomy) varies, and that the dog-human relationship represents a domain-specific cross-species version of the same machinery.

## Specification move

Three things unbundled before empirical work:

1. **Emotional contagion** — automatic affect-sharing without self-other distinction. Widespread across vertebrates. Adaptive for group defense.
2. **Empathy** — contagion *plus* self-other differentiation. Behavioral signature is consolation. Documented in great apes, canids, elephants, corvids, prairie voles.
3. **Theory of mind** — cognitive inference of another's mental states (beliefs, intentions, knowledge, perspectives). Standard test is the false-belief task. Children pass at ~age 4. Non-human animals robustly demonstrate perceptual perspective-taking; the false-belief component remains contested.

These three are often conflated in popular accounts and pulled apart in careful experiments.

## The mechanism for the deep-dive

*Coalition politics + perspective-taking + convergent evolution.* §10.3 walks the chimpanzee case (de Waal's *Chimpanzee Politics*; Hare-Call-Tomasello perspective-taking experiments; bonobo contrast) and then the dolphin case (Connor's three-tier alliances in Shark Bay). The convergence point: complex sociality with coalition tracking and perspective representation has evolved at least three times in mammals (primates, cetaceans, elephants) on anatomically dissimilar brains, with at least one cellular convergence (Von Economo neurons in anterior cingulate / frontal insula). One sentence: *the function shapes the substrate; sociality with political stakes selects for representation of others' representations, regardless of anatomy.*

## Central analogy

*Social intelligence as bookkeeping.* The animal in a complex social group is, computationally, doing bookkeeping — tracking who is allied with whom, who saw what, who owes whom, who can be trusted. Where the analogy breaks: a real ledger is explicit and indexed; the social brain's bookkeeping is implicit, distributed, and partial. The break is small and the analogy survives because it captures the right computational shape — the maintenance of a many-individual relational structure over time.

## Risks flagged

- **Three-capacity unbundling presented confidently** — emotional contagion / empathy / theory of mind. The literature has more nuance than the chapter conveys (e.g., empathy itself often subdivided into affective vs cognitive components; theory of mind further split into perceptual vs mental-state attribution). Compression is intentional for chapter scope; if review wants more granularity, it would expand §10.2 at the cost of trimming elsewhere.
- **Dunbar's number** treated honestly as contested. Specific 150 figure has wide confidence intervals in modern analyses; the broader correlation between neocortex ratio and group size has held up.
- **Von Economo neurons "Fast Lane Hypothesis"** included with a caveat in §10.3 that direct functional evidence is thinner than popular accounts suggest. The "Still puzzling" line names this directly.
- **Bonobo "costly help to unrelated out-group conspecifics" claim** is recent and the chapter treats it as well-supported; if review wants tighter sourcing, the Tan & Hare 2013 *PLOS ONE* paper or the more recent Krupenye-Hare lab follow-ups are the place.
- **Connor Shark Bay work** referenced compactly. The original three-tier alliance paper is Connor et al. 1992 *PNAS*, with subsequent refinements through 2022.
- **Brosnan & de Waal 2003 capuchin inequity** referenced only in passing; the food-frustration alternative interpretation is named but not deep-dived. The chapter's restraint here reflects genuine uncertainty in the literature.
